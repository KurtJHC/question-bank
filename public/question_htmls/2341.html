YOUR MOTHER SINGS

Your mother sings
an old song as she
hangs the wash. She looks around-
And when she is sure
no one is watching
(but you are watching) she lets the pigeons
she keeps at the bottom of her laundry basket
fly free—
Each has a note
in its beak. And now a pigeon
flies in your window, dies at your feet.
The note says: I live alone, please
come, please help me. But she doesn’t live
alone, your mother
is downstairs now
moving pots and pans, starting
dinner, singing
a song she sang,
you imagine, when you couldn’t sleep.
You hear her down there
Singing. You see the pigeon on the floor.

Michael Hettich

When I first encountered long division, it felt like trying to solve a riddle with invisible clues. I sat at my desk, pencil poised, copying the steps my teacher wrote on the board: divide, multiply, subtract, bring down. The process was mechanical and, at first, utterly meaningless to me. But something changed when I started thinking about why it worked. I began to see division not as a chore but as a process of repeated subtraction, of fairly sharing things out. Once I grasped that, I started drawing diagrams and visualising groups of things being split evenly. It was like a light had been switched on. I still remember the thrill of correctly dividing 189 by 9 and realising it meant there were exactly 21 groups. Learning arithmetic, for me, was not about memorising methods—it was about understanding patterns and relationships. I now teach younger students and try to avoid the trap of making maths just about answers. I want them to know the ‘why’ behind the steps. When students understand what numbers are doing and why we use certain methods, they’re more confident, less anxious, and better problem-solvers. Arithmetic is more than sums; it’s the foundation for logical thinking, fairness, and curiosity.

In my school days, arithmetic was a strict and serious business. We sat in rows, backs straight, reciting times tables aloud like a chant in a temple. I was terrified of making mistakes. My teacher, Mr Thomson, had a thin moustache and a thicker cane, and he didn’t tolerate wrong answers. We would work silently from textbooks filled with columns of sums, and when he called your name to come up to the board, it felt like a trial. You either emerged triumphant, or humiliated. I wasn’t bad at arithmetic, but I learned it with fear in my bones. Mistakes weren’t learning opportunities—they were failures. Looking back, it’s clear how damaging that approach was. It taught us accuracy but not confidence. Many of my classmates gave up on maths altogether, scarred by early experiences. Now, as a parent, I try to create a very different atmosphere when helping my children with their maths homework. We joke about problems, try different ways of solving them, and celebrate the journey as much as the answer. Arithmetic should never feel like punishment. It should be a place for exploration and small victories. I think Mr Thomson believed he was preparing us for life. In a way, he was—but not in the way he intended.

Teaching arithmetic in the digital age comes with a paradox. On the one hand, we have endless resources—videos, games, tutorials. On the other, I see more and more students who can punch numbers into a calculator but have no idea why their answer is right—or wrong. I once had a student tell me that 3 × 4 was 7. When I asked them to show me the answer using blocks or a drawing, they froze. Technology should enhance learning, not replace thinking. We risk raising a generation of button-pressers rather than problem-solvers. I use interactive apps in class, yes—but only after my students can explain concepts with their own hands and words. Arithmetic needs to start with concrete understanding: grouping, splitting, sharing, building. Only when students understand what numbers mean should they be trusted with shortcuts. There’s a kind of magic in watching a child figure out that 6 + 7 is the same as 7 + 6—and why. Mental flexibility matters more than speed. My goal as a teacher is not to produce walking calculators, but thoughtful learners who understand when and why to use maths in everyday life. That, to me, is the real purpose of arithmetic: making thinking visible and useful.
