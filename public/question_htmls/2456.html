Douglas Mawson



The name of Sir Douglas Mawson is synonymous with Australia’s Antarctic history. He was a true pioneer, venturing into unknown, hostile territory and almost losing his life in the cause of science. Born in Yorkshire, England, in 1882, Douglas was just two when his family moved to Sydney. He was educated at Fort Street Model School and then, from age 16, at the University of Sydney, where he graduated in Engineering and Science. (A)…………. It was here that he came under the influence of Professor Edgeworth David, the leading Australian geologist of the day.

Mawson’s early career was marked by a passion for geology and fieldwork. Before completing his science degree, he made a geological survey of the New Hebrides (Vanuatu) in 1903 and later produced a major work on the geology of Melanesia. (B)…………. Mawson’s first field trip to the Flinders Ranges took place in February 1906, where he studied in detail in later years. In 1907, he met Ernest Shackleton and was appointed physicist for Shackleton’s proposed British Antarctic Expedition. (C)…………. The following summer, Mawson, in a party led by David, journeyed on foot for over 2,000 km to the region of the South Magnetic Pole and back to the Ross Sea coast—the first to reach this elusive goal. The expedition almost ended in disaster, with David being crippled by the cold and Mawson having to be rescued from a crevasse.

Back in Australia, Mawson was determined to lead his own expedition to explore the Antarctic coast west of Cape Adare. He turned down an invitation from Robert Scott to join his South Pole sledging party, as Mawson had no interest in non-scientific endeavours. (D)…………. Mawson set up two Antarctic bases, one under Frank Wild and the main one, under his leadership, at Cape Denison in Commonwealth Bay.

Mawson himself led the Far Eastern Sledging Party with Belgrave Ninnis and Xavier Mertz. After five weeks and 500 km out, disaster struck: Ninnis, with one of the two sledges and most of the party’s supplies, was lost down an immense crevasse. (E)…………. Mawson survived after an epic solo journey of a month, during which he had to haul himself out of a deep crevasse on the end of a rope. It is one of the great polar stories of survival. Electric vehicles, or EVs, are cars powered by electricity rather than petrol or diesel. Their appeal lies in lower emissions, reduced fuel costs, and quieter driving. Advocates argue that EVs are vital in the fight against climate change, cutting pollution and dependence on fossil fuels. Governments in many countries offer incentives such as rebates and charging infrastructure to encourage their use. However, challenges remain. The batteries required for EVs are costly, rely on rare minerals, and take significant energy to produce. Critics also point out that electricity often comes from coal or gas, limiting the environmental benefits. Charging times can be inconvenient, and the range of some vehicles still lags behind petrol-powered cars. For these reasons, EVs are praised as a solution but also criticised as a technology still in transition. They represent progress, yet not perfection, in the journey toward sustainable transport.

Debate over electric vehicles is fierce in politics and society. Supporters emphasise cleaner cities, reduced oil dependency, and opportunities for green jobs. Detractors, however, highlight the high upfront cost of EVs, the strain on power grids, and the ethical questions of mining minerals for batteries. Some rural communities argue EVs are impractical due to limited charging networks, while urban areas often celebrate them as symbols of modern progress. Governments must weigh competing demands: how quickly to phase out petrol vehicles, how much to subsidise EV adoption, and how to balance industry interests with environmental goals. The discussion reflects broader questions about fairness, access, and responsibility. The debate about EVs is not only about cars, but also about how societies choose to balance progress with equity.
