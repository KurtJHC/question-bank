Chapter One: The New Arrival

Summary

In this first chapter, Mrs Koala has a brand-new baby koala! Every animal in the bushland is excited since Mrs Koala only gives birth to a baby every two years. Soon lots of different animals offer their help to Mr and Mrs Koala in looking after the baby. Mrs Koala agrees to let Angelina Wallaby take her baby for a walk in the bush in the evenings, until Mrs Snake attempts to hurt the baby. As the baby koala grows, he no longer fits in Mrs Koala’s pouch and has to ride her back. Mr Koala decides to name the baby, Blinky Bill. The Koalas arrange a christening (a Christian ceremony where a baby is officially given a name), but a flock of rude kookaburras almost ruin the lovely ceremony.

Read the poem below by Harriet Tubman then answer the questions.

 

The Conductor of the Underground
Railroad


The moment I was born, I thought I was going to be a slave every day
I worked in the cotton fields with no joy
It was a hard life with little joy and little sleep
I never whined, not even a peep
I am the conductor of the underground railroad.


When I was a teenager
I protected a slave from an overseer
The weight was thrown onto my head
It left me halfway dead
I am the conductor of the underground railroad.


I married John Tubman, a free man
Selling me down south, was his plan
My brothers were going to be sold too
The time to escape was overdue
I am the conductor of the underground railroad.


At night I fled, I was filled with dread
Will I be free or caught instead?
I took the underground railroad
I hid in the attics and basements of safe abodes
I am the conductor of the underground railroad.


I crossed the line
I was free and fine
But it wasn’t enough for me
I wanted my friends and family to be free
I am the conductor of the underground railroad.

 

I made more than 70 slaves free
I always had a gun on me
For protection and to encourage slaves not to stop
I drugged children so their cries won’t be non-stop
I am the conductor of the underground railroad.


The civil war broke out
That didn’t stop me from helping out
I spied on the confederate armies
At last we defeated our enemies
I am the conductor of the underground railroad.


I am Harriet Tubman
I took slaves away from the owners
I am buried with military honours
What I am towards slaves is what Moses is to Israelites
I am the conductor of the underground railroad.

For questions 1 – 6, choose the answer (A, B, C or D) which you think best answers the question.

Harriet’s personality can be described as
 
  1. pessimistic and stubborn.
  2. hopeful and resourceful.
  3. stoic and determined.
  4. sneaky and conniving.

Birches

When I see birches bend to left and right
Across the lines of straighter darker trees,
I like to think some boy’s been swinging them.
But swinging doesn’t bend them down to stay
As ice-storms do. Often you must have seen them
Loaded with ice a sunny winter morning
After a rain. They click upon themselves
As the breeze rises, and turn many-coloured
As the stir cracks and crazes their enamel.
Soon the sun’s warmth makes them shed crystal shells
Shattering and avalanching on the snow-crust—
Such heaps of broken glass to sweep away
You’d think the inner dome of heaven had fallen.
They are dragged to the withered bracken by the load,
And they seem not to break; though once they are bowed
So low for long, they never right themselves:
You may see their trunks arching in the woods
Years afterwards, trailing their leaves on the ground
Like girls on hands and knees that throw their hair
Before them over their heads to dry in the sun.

As the breeze rises, and turn many-coloured
As the stir cracks and crazes their enamel.
Soon the sun’s warmth makes them shed crystal shells
Shattering and avalanching on the snow-crust—
Such heaps of broken glass to sweep away
You’d think the inner dome of heaven had fallen.
They are dragged to the withered bracken by the load,
And they seem not to break; though once they are bowed
So low for long, they never right themselves:
You may see their trunks arching in the woods
Years afterwards, trailing their leaves on the ground
Like girls on hands and knees that throw their hair
Before them over their heads to dry in the sun.

Birches

When I see birches bend to left and right
Across the lines of straighter darker trees,
I like to think some boy’s been swinging them.
But swinging doesn’t bend them down to stay
As ice-storms do. Often you must have seen them
Loaded with ice a sunny winter morning
After a rain. They click upon themselves
As the breeze rises, and turn many-coloured
As the stir cracks and crazes their enamel.
Soon the sun’s warmth makes them shed crystal shells
Shattering and avalanching on the snow-crust—
Such heaps of broken glass to sweep away
You’d think the inner dome of heaven had fallen.
They are dragged to the withered bracken by the load,
And they seem not to break; though once they are bowed
So low for long, they never right themselves:
You may see their trunks arching in the woods
Years afterwards, trailing their leaves on the ground
Like girls on hands and knees that throw their hair
Before them over their heads to dry in the sun.

The Swagman


Old Bluey was a swaggie who
had travelled on the road,
with his worldly goods all bundled.
He had no fixed abode.


Possessions swung across his back   5
secure in calico,
a waterproof sheet and blanket
in his swag were set to go.


Dark blue it was to hide the dirt
with clothes, needle and thread,   10
a dog-eared photograph or two
and memories in his head.


He carried one old tattered book,
a small essential ration:
An old tin mug swung near his chest  15
in a carefree fashion.


In his hand there was a billy
or water in a can.
An axe was tucked in by the side
of Bluey, near his pan.   20


Sometimes he would walk forty miles
in just a single day
searching for some casual work,
supplies and food his pay.


He’d drive the cattle, drive the sheep,   25
or maybe just chop wood,
and do odd jobs to earn his crust.
He lived best as he could.


But woe betide a squatter who
denied the swaggie food,   30
he’d find his land was set alight,
his fence to be renewed.


Old Bluey was a battler who
had often found it tough,
but he’d never swap his lifestyle   35
as freedom was enough.

  2. a job.
  3. family.
