Memories
By Lenore Hetrick

Well, I’ve got to admit it was some Summer!
I don’t think I’ve ever had such wild fun.
Boy! I’ll have grand memories all winter long,
Now that vacation is vanished and done.

I can still see that wide, blue brook in the woods
With the pebbles shining so clean and bright,
And, oh! I know I will never forget
The way that the stars looked down on the night!

The smell of the thick woods after a rain!
The way that the trees dripped the last raindrops!
And the way that the sky turned blue as can be –
The blue it gets when the summer rain stops!

The best fun of all was out in the barn,
Up in the hayloft perched far overhead.
Down below all of the horses and cows
Chewed as they listened to the things that we said.

Oh, the memories I’ll have the whole winter long
Will be like a storybook that I will read
Again and again when the winter winds blow,
But to all those cold winds I will give little heed.

For I will be walking in green daisy meadows,
I will be wading in a wide, babbling brook,
And out of the window at the snow and the storm
I don’t think I’ll ever take one little look.

What season is the speaker remembering with joy?
 
  1. Autumn
  2. Winter
  3. Summer
  4. Spring

If I Were the Principal

By Kenn Nesbitt

If I were the principal, boy, things would change.
Our school would be fun, if a little bit strange.

We’d keep kangaroos in the classrooms as pets.
We’d travel to Tonga and learn to fly jets.

We’d get to make movies, and all become stars.
For field trips we’d blast off on rockets to Mars.

We’d learn to raise monsters and build time machines.
We’d surf on tsunamis in sleek submarines.

We’d learn to make robots with nuclear brains,
and dig up a dinosaur’s fossil remains.

We’d battle with pirates and plunder their gold.
We’d duel with dragons for treasures untold.

We’d practise some potions and magical spells
to stink up the schoolyard with sickening smells,
to make us invisible, eighty feet tall,
or turn into liquid or walk through a wall.

Yes, if I were Principal, that’s what we’d do.
We’d lock evil scientists up in the zoo,

while vanquishing villains and capturing crooks.
In other words, we would read many more books.

What is the speaker imagining?
 
  1. A normal school day
  2. Being the principal and changing school
  3. Leaving school forever
  4. Becoming a scientist

The Conductor of the Underground Railroad

By Harriet Tubman (inspired narrative poem)

The moment I was born,
I thought I was going to be a slave every day.
I worked in the cotton fields with no joy,
It was a hard life with little joy and little sleep,
I never whined, not even a peep,
I am the conductor of the Underground Railroad.

When I was a teenager
I protected a slave from an overseer,
The weight was thrown onto my head,
It left me halfway dead.
I am the conductor of the Underground Railroad.

I married John Tubman, a free man,
Selling me down south was his plan.
My brothers were going to be sold too,
The time to escape was overdue.
I am the conductor of the Underground Railroad.

At night I fled, I was filled with dread,
Will I be free or caught instead?
I took the Underground Railroad,
I hid in the attics and basements of safe abodes.
I am the conductor of the Underground Railroad.

I crossed the line, I was free and fine,
But it wasn’t enough for me.
I wanted my friends and family to be free.
I am the conductor of the Underground Railroad.

I made more than 70 slaves free,
I always had a gun on me,
For protection and to encourage slaves not to stop,
I drugged children so their cries won’t be non-stop.
I am the conductor of the Underground Railroad.

The Civil War broke out,
That didn’t stop me from helping out.
I spied on the Confederate armies,
At last we defeated our enemies.
I am the conductor of the Underground Railroad.

I am Harriet Tubman,
I took slaves away from the owners.
I am buried with military honours.
What I am towards slaves is what Moses is to Israelites,
I am the conductor of the Underground Railroad.

Who is speaking in the poem?
 
  1. A school teacher
  2. Harriet Tubman
  3. A train driver
  4. A soldier

Past, Present, Future

By June Kellum

Our past we can’t recapture.
It’s over and it’s done.
No way to recover time;
all those days are gone.

No amount of colouring
– painting – with lighter hue
can change the way things happened.
No way to make it true.

No use fretting over
– regretting – bygone days.
No need to get entangled
in memory’s purple haze.

…Or caught up in unravelling
(or of hoping we could do)
things that didn’t go the way
that we would want them to.

The future is uncertain.
No way for us to see
the things tomorrow’s holding,
for you…or for me!
NOW is what’s obtainable,
just moments here to share.
The future comes so quickly
unseen but waiting there.

Seize the present moments.
Grab all we can contain!
Our futures are elusive.
Our past we cannot chain!

What can we not do with the past according to the poem?
 
  1. Change it
  2. Forget it
  3. Talk about it
  4. Write about it

The Trains

by Judith Wright

Tunnelling through the night, the trains pass
in a splendour of power, with a sound like thunder
shaking the orchards, waking
the young from a dream, scattering like glass
the old men’s sleep, laying

a black trail over the still bloom of the orchards;
the trains go north with guns.

Strange primitive piece of flesh, the heart laid quiet
hearing their cry pierce through its thin-walled cave

recalls the forgotten tiger,

that changed us all –

the munching, the crawling,

like white petals falling!

so to skip high as towers,
and dip now to sweet fuel
from trembling bright flowers.
