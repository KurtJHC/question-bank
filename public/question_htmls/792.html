Going Along for the Ride

 

  In Australia a mother kangaroo hops along with a tiny head peeking out of a pocket on her belly. It’s an amusing sight. This pocket is called a pouch. For a baby kangaroo, rides in the mother’s pouch aren’t just for fun. 

  A baby kangaroo is called a joey. At birth it looks like a baked red bean. It is hairless, and its back legs are just little bumps. It can’t hear or see because its ears and eyes haven’t developed yet. To stay alive, the joey must quickly find its way into its mother’s pouch. She helps by licking a smooth path in her fur for the baby to follow. The joey has an acute sense of smell. Using this excellent sense of smell and strong arms, it reaches its mother’s pouch in about three minutes and drops inside. There it finds the milk that will keep it alive and growing for the next several months. 

  When the joey is about eight months old, the mother kangaroo relaxes her pouch muscles. Then the pouch droops open, and the joey tumbles out. It begins to explore the world outside. After looking around for a while, it goes back into its hiding place. 

  The joey soon learns to hop like its mother. When there is danger, the frightened joey jumps into the pouch. Its mother retreats at a speed of 15 to 20 miles per hour. If the mother senses that she might be caught, she hides the joey in the bushes. Then she hops away, trying to keep the pursuing person or animal away from her young. When she is no longer being followed, she goes back to get her joey. 

  A one-year-old joey is too big to stay in the pouch. Because the mother kangaroo usually has a baby every year, her pouch doesn’t stay empty very long.

  1.  A joey lives in its mother’s pouch until it is ready to live on its own.

  3.  Mother kangaroos can hop at speeds of 24 to 32 km per hour. 
  4.  A one-year-old joey hops into its other’s pouch only when there is danger.
