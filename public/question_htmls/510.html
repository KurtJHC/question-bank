HOW THE STOCK MARKET WORKS

 

A stock is defined as a share of ownership in a publicly-traded company that is traded on a stock exchange. Common stocks are called securities, sold to the public, that constitute an ownership stake in a corporation. They come in all sizes -- you can invest in a large, global company, like Apple (AAPL), or a smaller, micro-cap company that shows potential for profit.

When you buy a share of a stock, you automatically own a percentage of the firm, and an ownership stake of its assets. If you paid $100 for a share of stock, and the stock appreciates in value by, say, 10% during the period you own it, you've earned $10 on your stock investment.

That's the idea behind buying stocks -- to invest in solid, well-managed companies that turn a profit. A company that succeeds on those fronts stands a good chance of its stock price growing in value, while the company, in going public, makes use of the proceeds of the original stock sale to reach growth goals and manage operating expenses. The company can use the cash to invest in new markets, research new products, hire more workers and better advertise their products and services, among other things.

In most cases, it doesn't take much effort to buy stock shares and own a piece of a company. You would simply pay what the market is demanding (market price) for a particular stock, via a stockbroker either over the phone or via a digital device, and you're good to go. You'll receive confirmation of your purchase and can sell the stock whenever you like, hopefully for a profit.

As a partial company owner, being a stockholder presents perks - including sharing in company profits, voting on a company's board of directors and approving major changes at a firm, like a merger or an acquisition.

Why would a company ‘go public’?
  1.  To raise awareness and build the public profile of a company.
  2.  To allow more voting power on mergers and acquisitions.
  3.  To raise money from the proceeds of the original stock sale.
  4.  To sell stock for a profit.

Real Estate Agents

 

You're most likely handling the most substantial financial transaction of your life when you buy or sell a home, so it's of utmost importance that your agent is honest with you. Unfortunately, the real estate industry is saddled with a poor reputation because not all agents are honest.

Part of the problem is that vast numbers of unscrupulous people go into real estate thinking that the profession will provide them with a fast track to easy money. And it's not difficult to obtain a real estate license in many states. Professional standards and educational requirements are at a minimum in some jurisdictions.

Most agents are more trustworthy than they're given credit for. They survive on repeat business, so they want and need happy clients. The Realtor Code of Ethics prohibits unethical behavior on top of that. But a few dishonest agents can still slip through.

 

When and How Do Agents Lie?

Agents mostly lie about themselves. They might misrepresent their experience or their credentials on their websites or blogs. Some spend inordinate amounts of time posting online to obtain maximum search engine exposure. Others pay for commercial rights and buy ads to rank above search results in Google and Yahoo.

So you might wonder—and rightly so—if you can trust an agent's website.

You can find out just about anything you need to know about a real estate agent's honesty with a little detective work. You can assess whether agents are puffing or giving you a false picture of their abilities, strengths, and background. Be alert when you're looking at an agent's website or blog!

Why does the real estate industry generally have a bad reputation?
  1.  There is not much higher education required to be a real estate agent. 
  2.  Real estate agents tend to exaggerate how great a house or property is.
  3.  A few dishonest real estate agents have given the whole industry a bad name.
  4.  The real estate industry does not have a bad reputation; most agents are honest.
