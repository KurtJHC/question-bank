My Room 

 

My room had no treasures in it until last Thursday night. But it does now. It’s an object. Small, scrolled silver with a fine hinge. Good to hold in your hand or to press against your hot cheek.

 

My grandfather came to visit last Thursday night and he gave gifts to my sister Nellie and to me. He gave Nellie an old silver hairbrush that had been my Grandma’s. She was really pleased with it and I think she was a bit sorry for me with my gift – the silver scrolled object. But I liked my gift very much. And when Nellie had gone off to the movies, my grandfather told me a story that made me like it even more.

 

Once, when he was a boy, my grandfather’s dad, Alex had been the best fisherman on Blackheath Beach. Alex went fishing there, often alone. There was a smart young man from Brisbane who came there on holiday. He was determined to go fishing, though he knew nothing about it and nagged and nagged Alex until he agreed to take him out in his boat, even though did he not particularly like the arrogant bloke. 

 

They fished the afternoon away with Danny getting annoyed as Alex caught many fish and he hardly anything worthwhile. Late in the afternoon, Alex began to pull up anchor. 

 

‘There’ll be a storm for sure,’ he told Danny. Danny was annoyed at having caught so few fish. 

 

‘I’m paying you well and you agreed to six hours,’ he said.

 

‘You can have your money back, but I’m not going to stay out here in a storm,’ Alex replied. 

 

‘Well just another half an hour,’ Danny wheedled.

 

‘It’ll be hard work getting back in. I can promise you,’ Alex replied. 

 

‘Scared of water, are you?’ 

 

‘No, just respectful,’ said Alex quietly. Of course, Alex’s prediction was right. A storm blew up quickly and they had a terrible struggle to bring the little boat in. It veered off near the headland in a raging wind and carried them towards the deserted Redhead Beach. But short of the surf the boat overturned and filled and went under in a matter of minutes. Now Alex was a fisherman and a swimmer but Danny, as it turns out was neither. He clung to Alex and it was all Alex could do to drag the heavy fellow through the wild surf. Alex, who was as determined as he was strong, was carrying the now inert body and dragged it through the waves and onto the beach at Redhead. 

 

Alex was exhausted and realised that if he left the unconscious man on the beach he would surely die. But Alex was also a practical man. He saw what was needed to be done and set about doing it. Within minutes he’d found a shelter under a ledge of rock for Danny who was now shivering and moaning. He collected driftwood and the brittle bushes growing amongst the scattered rocks, and made a neat pile against the cliff face. 

 

And then he took from his sodden shirt pocket a small shiny object made of scrolled silver that had a hinged top. It sprung open, revealing tinder dry matches inside. Enough of them to light an unwilling spluttering fire that turned slowly, slowly into a healthy warming fire and that finally blazed with a heat that breathed life back into Danny’s frozen body and warmed Alex as he fed the fire the whole night long. They were found by neighbours who came looking for them the next day when it was calm. ‘Storm stopped you two last night, did it? Had a tough time of it, eh?’ they asked. 

 

‘Nothing we couldn’t handle,’ Danny blurted out.  

 

When Grandad offered me the gift, he said, ‘My father, Alex gave me this silver match case as I’m giving it to you, Sally.’ And he added, ‘Treasure it, because it saved two men’s lives. And one of them was forever grateful.’ 

 

My room had no treasure in it until last Thursday night. But it does now.
