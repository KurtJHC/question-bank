Gallipoli

 

On 25 April 1915, Australian soldiers landed at what is now called Anzac Cove on the Gallipoli Peninsula. For the vast majority of the 16,000 Australians and New Zealanders who landed on that first day, this was their first experience of combat. 1 ………… . The Gallipoli campaign was hence, a military failure. Life was very dear, but life was not worth living unless they could be true to their idea of Australian manhood.”

 

The British had been trying to force their way through the narrow straits known as the Dardanelles to capture Istanbul in Turkey. Chris Barnard – the first surgeon to perform a heart transplant 

 

In 1967, an obscure cardiac surgeon in South Africa became an instant household name. The name Christiaan Barnard was indelibly linked to a feat once thought impossible – transplanting the human heart. Christiaan Barnard was born in 1922 in South Africa. He graduated in 1946 from the University of Cape Town Medical School. After an internship at Groote Schuur Hospital in Cape Town, he worked as a general practitioner in the rural town of Ceres. In 1951, Barnard won a scholarship for postgraduate training in cardiothoracic surgery at the University of Minnesota in the United States (US). There, he learnt to surgically correct congenital heart defects. 1 ………… . There he established Groote Schuur Hospital’s first heart unit and soon headed its Division of Cardiothoracic Surgery.

 

In 1964, Barnard began working with canine kidney transplants. He spent three months with kidney transplant surgeon David Hume at the Medical College of Virginia in the US. 2 ………… . He also began experimenting with canine heart transplants with the help of gifted laboratory technician Hamilton Naki, a South African man. Near the end of the 19th century, famed British physician, Sir James Paget was quoted as saying: “Surgery of the heart has probably reached the limits set by nature to all surgery.” Yet, less than a century later, pioneering research in heart transplant surgery had begun in earnest. South Africa raced to become the first in line. 

 

For his first attempt at a heart transplant, Barnard selected as the recipient Louis Washkansky, a 54-year-old grocer with severe coronary artery disease. Then on 2 December 1967, opportunity came with the tragedy of 25-year-old Denise Darvall. 3 ………… . Barnard’s transplant team gained permission from Darvall’s father to use her heart. The entire procedure took nine hours and involved a team of 30 professionals. The recipient patient only lived 18 days with his new heart, succumbing to pneumonia while on a hefty immunosuppressive regimen. His short survival prompted doubts about the true value of the procedure. Critics felt Barnard did not deserve the acclaim when Dr Shumway (American) had done the bulk of the background research.

 

Barnard became an overnight international celebrity. A New York Times article captured public sentiment with these words: “The world was startled to learn that the human heart, traditional seat of the emotions, home of the soul, symbol of love, could be transplanted from one person to another as though it were nothing more than a complicated piece of plumbing.” Barnard’s picture graced the covers of magazines such as Time, and he accepted invitations worldwide to meet dignitaries and heads of states. One of his colleagues summed up Barnard’s complex character in these words: “He was then, at once, rough-at-the-edges poor boy and charming sophisticate, democrat and tyrant, selfless healer and boorish egotist, lover and Don Juan, shrewd but naive acceptor of glitterati adulation. 4 ………… .

 

Barnard’s next transplant candidate was 59-year-old dentist, Philip Blaiberg. He modified the surgical technique and added antilymphocyte serum to the posttransplant immunosuppressive procedure. Blaiberg lived for 19 months after his transplant. 5 ………… . However, Blaiberg’s daughter asserted after his death, “My father’s life was hell after the transplant. He suffered terribly all the time, but the world was cheering for a medical miracle and did not know.” 

 

Barnard continued to perform transplants, but only ten were done in the following five years. His fifth patient lived for another 13 years and his sixth 23 years. He then took on heterotopic transplantation, where only the diseased portion of the recipient’s heart was removed. He performed some 49 procedures over a nine-year period, but the results were mixed. 6 ………… . Heart transplant recipients today enjoy a oneyear survival rate of over 80% and five-year survival of about 70%.
