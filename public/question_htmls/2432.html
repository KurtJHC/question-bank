As American as . . . Pizza Pie?
by Michael Demchsak

1     In America, the pizza industry makes more than 30 billion dollars a year. Americans eat 350 slices of pizza per second. A pizza that would satisfy the cravings of the American public each day would cover a 100-acre field. Among kids ages 3–11, pizza is reported to be the first choice for either lunch or dinner.

2     Consequently, it is not surprising that pizza seems ready to take the title of “America’s favorite food” away from the hamburger. 

3     The success achieved by the pizza pie is fairly amazing. After all, pizza was an ethnic food found only in big-city Italian neighborhoods until around 60 years ago. 

Ancient History 
4     In the area around the Mediterranean Sea, people have been baking flat breads, usually covered with herbs, for thousands of years. In fact, the Latin word picea refers to bread blackening in an oven. It may be the origin of the term pizza. The flat breads baked by these Mediterranean people, especially the Italians, were sometimes topped with cheese or vegetables. The result was fairly close to being pizza. But these culinary efforts were not what most of us think of when we hear the word pizza.

5     Two of the essential ingredients—the crust and the cheese—were in place. It is hard to imagine pizza without tomato sauce, but the tomato is not native to the Mediterranean. It would be many years before this third crucial ingredient would be available. The tomato is a New World plant. It would not have been available in Europe until the 16th century, when Spanish explorers brought it back from Mexico. 

More Recent History 
6     In the 16th and 17th centuries, the city of Naples in Italy began producing the ancestors of the tasty treats we now call pizzas. Vendors carried pizzas through the streets in metal stoves worn on their heads. This practice of taking the product to the customer helped increase the popularity of pizza. The first sit-down pizzeria, the Antica Pizzeria Port’Alba, opened in 1830. It continues to operate to this day.

7     It was not until 1889 that pizza had its great moment in history. A restaurant owner in Naples was asked to make a dish in honor of the visiting Queen Margherita. He decided to recreate a version of the Italian flag on crusty flat bread. Tomatoes, mozzarella cheese and a puree of basil formed the red, white and green of the Italian flag. The dish was a giant hit. In fact, visitors to Naples today can still see a royal thank you note from June 1889 written by Galli Camillo, the head of the table of the royal household of Italy.

8     Italian immigrants brought their love of pizza to the United States. It was popular in Italian neighborhoods in the early 20th century. The first pizzeria in the United States opened in 1905 in New York City. For a time, the popularity of pizza remained restricted mostly to cities where large groups of Italians lived. But servicemen who had been stationed in Italy during World War II (1941–1945) sparked a new enthusiasm for pizza as they returned home. That spark obviously turned into a wildfire. 

Getting Creative with Pizza 
9     Pizza is an incredibly versatile dish. Over time, modifications to pizza and its ingredients have become common. Purists can debate whether or not a particular combination is really pizza. But personal and cultural preferences guarantee that pizza will vary widely—and wildly— depending where in the world it is found. At the moment, pizza can be eaten in this country with regular hand-tossed, deep-dish, or ultra-thin crust. Seemingly unusual ingredients are not very unusual at all. For example, Americans with a taste for something different can often ask for a pizza topped with anything from pineapple to sauerkraut. In other lands, the choices may be even more interesting. There are squid pizzas in Japan and mackerel pizzas in Russia. There are coconut pizzas in Costa Rica and curry pizzas in Pakistan.

Pizza Today 
10     In America today, there are approximately 61,000 pizzerias. Pizza is so common that it is hard to imagine that there are people today who grew up in a time when many Americans had never heard of pizza or seen a slice of this food favorite. It seems almost sad. But those times are gone. Now it can be claimed that America is the pizza capital of the world. But . . . shhh! Don’t tell Naples.

  3. They had a top crust and a bottom crust.
  4. They were topped with cheese.

     all living Nobel Prize winners signed a

5    this stark statement: ‘Human beings and

10  our practices put at serious risk the
     future we wish for human society.’

15  unusual alarm call. In biological terms,
     the globe is experiencing an ecoholocaust,

25    But our obsession with economics has

30  ups. We can’t afford to protect the

        human-created environment of big
        cities where it’s easy to believe the
80    illusion that we have escaped our
        biological dependence on the
        natural world.
        The explosive increase in
         information shatters the world into
85     fragments devoid of the history
        or context that might explain
        their relevance, importance or
        significance. In order to attract
        attention, stories or reports become
90    shorter and increasingly shrill,
        sensational or violent.

        scientists and technologists — will
        resolve our problems is simply
95    unwarranted. While technology
        can be impressive, our knowledge
        of the complexity of the real world

        no idea how to replace thousands
100     of species now extinct or repair the
        ozone layer.

        to live rich and fulfilling lives? I believe
        you cannot separate environmental
105    and social needs. Hungry people will
       not care if their actions endanger an
       edible species or an important habitat.
       Unemployment, injustice or insecurity
       lead to desperation and the need to
110    survive at all costs.

120    knowledge that there are cosmic forces

125    now, we seem to be trying to squeeze

Rescue at Pea Island
by Tracey E. Fern

Station Keeper Richard Etheridge peered from the lookout tower of the Pea Island life-saving station. Fog swirled around his face, and it was pitch-dark. Huge waves crashed onto the beach, and hurricane-force winds hurled sand and sea spray into the air.

On that night back in October 1896, the worst storm in almost fifty years was battering the North Carolina coast. Moments earlier, one of Etheridge’s surfmen, Theodore Meekins, had spotted a faint red glimmer in the distance. Could it have been a flare from a ship in distress?

Etheridge quickly lit a flare and held it high over his head until the flame died out. Then he saw it—a dull red light flickered to the south. That could mean only one thing: shipwreck.

The Keeper of the Station
Rescuing ships in distress was nothing new for Etheridge. He had been keeper of this life-saving station since 1880. Etheridge had grown up swimming, fishing, and boating on nearby Roanoke Island. He eventually became known as one of the best surfmen and lifesavers in North Carolina. But Etheridge was also known for something else: he was the Life-Saving Service’s first African American station chief.

White surfmen refused to work under Etheridge. So Etheridge and the Life-Saving Service recruited a crew of African American rescuers to work with him. 

Etheridge’s men trained for ten hours a day, seven days a week. They swam in the rough surf and patrolled the beach. They performed life-saving drills and tugged their 1,500-pound surfboat and equipment for miles through the sand. But the wreck on the night of October 1896 put all of the crew’s practice to the test.

Rescuing the Wreck
As soon as Etheridge and Meekins spotted the red flare, they woke up the other five surfmen on duty. The crew quickly hitched up a team of mules. Then they pushed and pulled their surfboat and equipment cart down the beach for two miles.

“The storm was raging fearfully,” Etheridge later wrote, “and . . . the team was often brought to a standstill by the sweeping current.” But the crew kept trudging through the knee-deep sand and icy water. Finally, they spotted the wreck.

The schooner E.S. Newman had blown aground 30 yards offshore. The storm had ripped off the ship’s sails and demolished her deck. Now the battered ship was rolling and tossing in the surf. All nine of the passengers and crew, including the captain’s wife and three-year-old child, were trapped onboard.

“It seemed impossible under such circumstances to render any assistance,” Etheridge later wrote.

But unless someone did something soon, the passengers would probably be swept out to sea, die from exposure, or be crushed when the Newman broke apart in the surf. So Etheridge came up with a daring plan.

His men would try to swim to the ship. Meekins and another surfman volunteered for the first attempt. They lashed themselves together with a rope. Then they grabbed one end of a life-saving line while the rest of the crew held the other end of the line securely to the beach.

Meekins and the other surfman slowly waded farther out into the chilly water. They struggled through the currents and against the undertow in total darkness.

Onboard the Newman, the captain lowered a ladder over the side of the broken ship. Finally the surfmen reached the ship.

“The voice of gladden[ed] hearts greeted the arrival of the station crew,” Etheridge later wrote.

One passenger climbed carefully down the ladder. Meekins knotted the end of the rope around him. Slowly they carried him through the churning water to safety. Then two other surfmen headed back to the Newman.

Next, they carried the child to shore. Then they returned to rescue the captain’s wife. Again and again, the surfmen took turns swimming in pairs through the sea to the wreck. After six grueling hours, the Newman was destroyed, but all nine passengers were safely onshore.

Life After the Rescue
The captain of the Newman was so grateful that he gave Etheridge and his crew the ship’s name board. But for many years, this was the only award that the surfmen received. Pea Island Station was closed in 1947, and everyone seemed to forget the courageous crew.

Finally, in 1996 Etheridge and his men were awarded the Coast Guard’s highest honor, the Gold Lifesaving Medal, for their “extreme and heroic daring.” One hundred years after the Newman rescue, the men of the Pea Island Station were recognized for what they were—heroes.

[^cstable^]Coming to the Crew’s Rescue

Kate Burkart, an eighth-grader from North Carolina, helped to “rescue” the Pea Island crew. She wrote letters to her senator and President Clinton.

With Coast Guard officer Stephen Rochon and graduate students David Wright and David Zoby, Burkart submitted a report to the Coast Guard about the crew.

Their hard work paid off. Burkart was at the Navy Memorial when the Gold Lifesaving Medal was awarded to the Pea Island crew. [^/cstable^]
