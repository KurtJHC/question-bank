The heavy-set man came at Stig with a rush.
 
1     His arms were held out ahead of him as if ready for an embrace, his fingers curled and ready to grip. He was taller than Stig, and perhaps twelve kilograms heavier. His chest and upper body were thickly muscled. Stig could see a light sheen of oil covering his arms, and he had time to think that this was not quite in the spirit of the contest.
 
2     He braced himself, and their two bodies came together with a solid WHUMP of flesh meeting flesh. If his attacker had hoped to drive the wind out of Stig with the impact, his aim was thwarted. The young warrior had tensed his muscles ready for the hit. He stepped back half a pace, but otherwise remained steady.
 
3     Let him come to you, Thorn had told him. See what he’s got before you start.
 
4     What he had was not particularly skillful or unexpected. He wrapped his arms around Stig’s waist in a clumsy bear hug and, beginning to lean back, attempted to lift him off the ground, so that he could apply pressure to the kidneys and lungs as Stig hung helpless in his embrace.
 
5     But Stig wasn’t ready to be helpless—and he’d watched the man use this very tactic in a previous bout. As he felt the man’s arms wrap around him, and was drawn in tight against him, Stig rammed his right hand, palm open, under the man’s chin, locking his elbow tightly in a right angle and supporting his right arm with his left hand. The arm formed a rigid, unyielding barrier against the man’s attempts to lift Stig’s feet off the sand of the arena. In effect, as long as Stig could keep his right arm locked, the man was trying to lift himself off the ground along with his opponent.
 
6     The larger man grunted with the effort, trying to twist his chin away from Stig’s iron grip. But Stig maintained the pressure and his opponent was caught in a stalemate. The more he heaved and strained, the more he exhausted himself. Yet he lacked the imagi­nation or speed of thought to change the tactic. It had always worked for him before. It should work for him now.
 
7     Except, in previous bouts, his opponents hadn’t been ready for the hold. And if they were, they had no effective counter to it.
Why did Stig think that light sheen of oil ‘was not quite in the spirit of the contest’ (Paragraph 1)?
  1.  The oil was impractical for the opponent’s moves.
  2.  The shine of the oil made the opponent more attractive.
  3.  Using oil was an unfair tactic to make the opponent more difficult to attack.
  4.  The contest had forbidden the use of oil.
