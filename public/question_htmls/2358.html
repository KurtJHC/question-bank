I roll over and wake. The light
is like a divorce photograph. And a sharp
bird-note
pierces beneath the verandah’s deep

frontal lobe. Sit up to face
the sky, an infinite
cruel calm. Trees in this place
feathered bones. And the cliff, a brute

relic, re-emerging over
tin roofs. This light, with its copious dust
swirling, burrows for cover
indolents. There, underside of a crust,

one’s sheet. Another alarmed call. That alert
sentry, a weapon in its face, was here:
it came in the verandah’s slit,
and has leapt again on the swollen air.

Rosemary Dobso

In Māori culture, wood is deeply sacred and spiritually powerful, serving as both a practical material and a vessel of ancestral connection. Known as rākau, wood is central to carving traditions (whakairo), which are not merely decorative but rich in symbolism and genealogy. Māori carvers (tohunga whakairo) use native trees like tōtara, kauri, and rimu to create intricate patterns that tell stories of gods (atua), ancestors (tīpuna), and tribal history. Meeting houses (wharenui) are among the most significant wooden structures in Māori culture. Every part of the wharenui represents a part of an ancestor’s body — the ridgepole as the spine, the rafters as ribs — transforming the building into a living embodiment of tribal lineage. Entering a wharenui is like stepping into a sacred body, where wood, carvings, and memory become one. Wood is also used to craft weapons (taiaha), canoes (waka), and ceremonial objects, each shaped with reverence and accompanied by spiritual rituals. Trees are seen as ancestors themselves, and their felling is often marked by karakia (prayers) to acknowledge their spirit and request safe passage for their transformation. For Māori, wood is not inert — it is living, breathing history. It links past and present, earth and sky, people and gods, carving Māori identity into the heart of the natural world.

Even in today’s high-tech world, our modern lifestyle still relies heavily on trees and the materials they provide. Wood, one of the oldest resources used by humans, remains essential in construction, furniture, and everyday products. From the framing of homes and schools to the desks we work at and the flooring beneath our feet, wood offers durability, flexibility, and a lower carbon footprint compared to many synthetic materials — especially when responsibly sourced through sustainable forestry practices. Beyond timber, trees provide countless materials that support daily life. Paper, derived from wood pulp, is used not only for books, notebooks, newspapers, and packaging, but also in tissues, toilet paper, food cartons, and even in specialty uses like filter paper for tea bags and laboratory settings. Despite the digital shift, paper remains vital in education, healthcare, and industry. Tree resins such as rosin and turpentine, extracted from pines, are essential in products like varnishes, soaps, chewing gum, and even violin bows. Trees also contribute to packaging innovations. Cardboard, made from layers of paperboard, is now used in everything from Amazon deliveries to fast-food containers, replacing plastic foam and single-use plastic wraps. New technologies are transforming cellulose — the main component of plant cell walls — into biodegradable plastics, wearable fabrics like lyocell, and even transparent wood for use in windows and solar panels.

Trees are logged all over the world to produce paper and other essential materials. The process begins with the careful selection and felling of trees, often from managed forests. While this process supplies many of the materials we rely on daily, it does come with environmental costs. Large-scale logging can lead to deforestation, habitat loss, and reduced biodiversity if not managed sustainably. The production of paper also uses significant amounts of water and energy.

To reduce these impacts, many paper companies now use recycled paper, plant new trees to replace those cut, and apply cleaner, more efficient manufacturing methods. Still, balancing human needs with environmental protection remains a crucial challenge.

Logging, when carried out irresponsibly and on a massive scale, is one of the most destructive forces facing our planet. Entire forests — rich, ancient ecosystems that took centuries to grow — are being reduced to stumps in a matter of days. With each tree felled, we edge closer to ecological collapse. Wildlife is driven out or killed, their habitats destroyed beyond repair. Iconic species such as orangutans, jaguars, and countless insects face extinction, their homes vanishing before they have a chance to adapt or escape. But the damage doesn’t stop there. Trees are vital to absorbing carbon dioxide and regulating Earth’s climate. When they are logged, the carbon they’ve stored for decades is released into the atmosphere, supercharging global warming. The result? More extreme weather, rising seas, and a rapidly changing planet. Ravaged forests leave behind scarred land — dry, eroded, and unable to support life. Rivers choke with mud and chemicals, drinking water is poisoned, and entire communities suffer. If unsustainable logging continues, the Earth’s lungs will be lost — and with them, our future.
