What is wrong with this conclusion?
 
  1. Ants do not normally try to carry children, since they are focused on transporting food or nest materials.
  2. Ants often move big objects by working together, so the strength of one ant does not simply scale up to carrying a child.
  3. Larger animals cannot carry the same multiples of weight because their bodies scale differently.
  4. Ants live in colonies, not as individuals, so the ants work together as a team.

73545

Lucy reads that larger ant colonies evacuate their nests only when the temperature rises above 36°C, while smaller colonies evacuate at lower temperatures. She concludes:

“This proves that individual ants in larger colonies can tolerate more heat than those in smaller colonies.”

 

What is the reasoning error here?
 
  1. Ants do not rely only on their antennae to detect temperature, so assuming evacuation differences come from individual sensing is misleading.
  2.  The difference arises from the size of the group and collective decision-making, not from each ant’s personal tolerance to heat.
  3. Larger colonies are often found in different locations that may already be warmer, so the evacuation point could reflect the environment rather than physiology.
  4. Smaller colonies sometimes appear less stable because their queens are more vulnerable, so their evacuation at lower temperatures may be linked to colony structure, not heat tolerance.
