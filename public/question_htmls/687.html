BRIAN MAY

 

Queen couldn't possibly have been Queen without Freddie Mercury, nor could it have been Queen without Brian May. Thanks not least to the recent biopic, Bohemian Rhapsody, the band's already larger-than-life lead singer has become even larger still. But its guitarist, despite the film's surface treatment of his character, is in his own way an equally implausible figure. Not only did he show musical promise early, forming his first group while still at school, he also got his A Levels in physics, mathematics, and applied mathematics, going on to earn a Bachelor of Science in Physics with honours at Imperial College London.

 

Naturally, May then went for his PhD, continuing at Imperial College where he studied the velocity of, and light reflected by, interplanetary dust in the Solar System. He began the program in 1970, but "in 1974, when Queen was but a princess in its infancy, May chose to abandon his doctorate studies to focus on the band in their quest to conquer the world." So wrote The Telegraph's Felix Lowe in 2007, the year the by-then 60-year-old (and long world-famous) rocker finally handed in his thesis. "The 48,000-word tome, Radial Velocities in the Zodiacal Dust Cloud, which sounds suspiciously like a Spinal Tap LP, was stored in the loft of his home in Surrey." 

 

In his abstract, May notes the unusually long period of study as 1970-2007, made possible by the fact that little other research had been done in this particular subject area during Queen's reign on the charts and thereafter. Still, he had catching up to do, including observational work in Tenerife. Since being awarded his doctorate, May's scientific activities have continued, as have his musical ones and other pursuits besides, such as animal-rights activism and stereography. (Sometimes these intersect: the 2017 photobook Queen in 3-D, for example, uses a VR viewing device of May's own design.) The next time you meet a youngster dithering over whether to go into astrophysics or found one of the most successful rock bands of all time, point them to May's example and let them know doing both isn't without precedent.

  4.  May completed his first PhD before leaving university to pursue rock stardom.

Read the four extracts below about thunderstorms.

For questions 21 to 25, choose the option (A, B, C or D) which you think best answers the question.

Which extract…

Introduces the storm in a metaphorical way?
 

  1.  Extract A

    The red and pink clad sky darkened as the evening clouds overtook the last glimpse of sunlight. Rain began to sprinkle on the dry ground as the winds picked up, blowing leaves and debris across the field below. Wild horses ran to the relative comfort of the tree line as the first crashes of thunder rolled through the valley. Throughout the night, strikes of lightning sent rippling waves of thunder crashing and bouncing off the canyon walls, before finally cascading down to the gorging river below. The rain threatened to wash away the mountain, as newly formed streams of water trickled through the cracks in the rocks. 

  2.  Extract B

    You always know when the storm's coming. You can feel the air get warmer, the air get more humid, and the skies look dark. You hear a loud BOOM from far away, the clap of thunder. Clouds roll in quickly and start to rain. It's never a light drizzle, but a downpour. You see the sky light up for less than a quarter of a second, the first strike of lightning. The skies get even darker, the rain gets a lot heavier, and lightning strikes more often. As you would expect, as it ends, the lightning and rain start to happen less frequently. You can see a light in the distance, the end of the storm. The sun shining brightly as the last drops of rain fall. Up in the blue sky, a magnificent display of color in the form of an amazing rainbow. As violent as storms can be, a thunderstorm can almost always be a calming experience.

  3.  Extract C

    Three drops of rain freckled her cheeks, and suddenly it came all at once, a constant patter on her nose and arms and hair, rolling down her neck and tracing her spine. Thunder rumbled and she sank to the ground, feeling the water seep into her jeans. She lay back and let her hair tangle with the grass, watching the leaves above her, those harbingers of their own destruction, be whipped by cords of rain and lose their grip on the branches. One settled just at her fingertips and she grasped onto it, rolling onto her stomach to examine its network of veins, as her tongue collected the raindrops that trailed down her face. The rain drummed a muffled thudding on the hard-packed dirt, she could feel it beating with a desperate passion a path for the rivulets and puddles she knew would come, resulting in the stench of stagnant water in a few days’ time. The wind howled like a cry of loss. It was a grief beyond her understanding, and she felt a certain shame to look up at that marbled sky, green and purple and brown. Splashed across it was a wild and primitive pain too enormous for her to grasp, but the storm smeared rain like tears down her face, as though inviting her to share in it, to swallow her whole and envelop her in its misery.

  4.  Extract D

    It started with the air. When it started it was almost like static--not gentle, but not painful. It was an itch, and it grew stronger and stronger. The static became sparks and we could feel it at our fingertips. And it all felt heavy--gravity coming up from the earth and into the air. The air was static, and heavy and then sticky. We knew. 

    The air was followed by the wind. It wasn't a blowing wind--not a wind for kites or gliders. No... this wind had sharp edges. It made us stagger when we walked and nauseous when we stood still.
