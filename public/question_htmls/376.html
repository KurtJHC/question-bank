Questions 13 - 15 relate to the information text, “How Bridges are Built Over Water.”

 

How Bridges Are Built Over Water

 

Almost everyone travels every day and you may pass a bridge on your travels. Bridges are the connecting link between two lands separated by a water body. Do you ever think how the pillars are built in the water bodies to construct the bridge?

Bridges are built over water by different methods, depending on the water level and soil quality.

For bridges built in shallow water, the foundations are laid by filling the location for a temporary period, over which piers (a type of pillar) can be built. Otherwise when the soil is not favourable in the top layer for constructing a bridge, temporary rigs are erected and piles are constructed deep inside the river bed. Then the bridge can be built either by taking support from the already constructed piers, or by temporary platforms / mud-islands in the water, or by erecting barges.

For bridges over deep water bodies (rivers or seas), the cofferdam technique is used. In this method, a wall enclosing an area is built inside the water and water is continuously pumped out from the area. After that, the foundation of the bridge (pillars) are constructed inside the cofferdam. As the construction place is sea or river itself, 24-hour monitoring is required outside the cofferdam to check high tides of water. Bridges built using this technique are strong and carry heavy loads.

The final technique is called Case Drilling. This is the most advanced technique. A water tight chamber keeps the water away with the help of air pressure. Then a sealed tube chamber is fitted inside the chamber. After that, a long drill is put inside the tube and then the drilling process starts. Water filled during this process is pumped out. After that a case is inserted into the hole which is drilled inside to give extra support. In this way, a stable frame is created. This frame is filled with concrete. Then pillars are built and upon those, the bridge.
