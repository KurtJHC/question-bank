Our American Trees
by Robert C. Birkby


     The largest and oldest living things on earth are trees. Over a thousand kinds grow in the United States, and thousands more flourish in other parts of the world. Trees play a very important role in the health of the environment and in the lives of people. The following are a few of the benefits we
enjoy from trees:

  • Trees pump oxygen into the air. The great forests of the world are essential for purifying the air and maintaining the right balance of oxygen in the atmosphere.
  • Leaves on branches and on the ground slow falling rain so that it can seep into the earth. Roots keep dirt from washing away. Fallen leaves decay in the soil and enrich it.
  • Trees provide shelter and food for wildlife. A forest is a complicated web of relationships among hundreds of types of plants and animals. That diversity helps all species survive. Strong forests increase the stability of the environment.
  • Trees provide shelter and food for humans, too. Lumber goes into homes. Fruits, berries, nuts, and oils feed us. Other products of trees include paper, rubber, spices, and medicines.
  • Shaded yards and city parks offer us places close to home where we can relax and play. Hiking and camping let us explore vast forests and wilderness areas.

 

Parts of a Tree

     Roots. Much of a tree is out of sight beneath the ground. Growing very quickly, thousands of tiny root hairs push through the earth, absorbing moisture and sending it up into the tree. A root system is also the anchor holding a tree upright even in storms and high winds.

     Trunks. The bark on a tree trunk gives the plant a tough outer armor. Sapwood underneath the bark transports moisture from the roots up to the leaves. Between the sapwood and bark is a thin layer of tissue called the cambium layer. It channels food produced by the leaves down into the tree trunk and the roots. In the center of the trunk is hardened sapwood called heartwood that gives the tree strength. Each year the cambium builds a new layer of sapwood around the trunk. Count the rings formed by the layers and you’ll know how many years a tree was alive.

     Leaves. The green chlorophyll in the leaves draws power from sunlight and converts carbon dioxide in the air into nutrients for the tree. Called photosynthesis, this process returns oxygen to the atmosphere. It is an important way for the air to be cleaned and refreshed with oxygen.

     The two large groups of American trees are conifer trees and broadleaf trees. Conifers, also known as evergreens, have needlelike or scalelike leaves that usually stay on the trees for several years. Broadleaf trees have flat leaves that generally fall off in the autumn.

The Colour of Sunshine

By David Metzenthen

 

It rains all day. I go to Treymouth State School, but Stewie, my brother, is in the navy. Stewie's ship is grey, the colour of this sky. His ship is a destroyer. Stewie is a cook. Sometimes I wish he was something better than a cook. If I was on a destroyer, I would not be a cook. I would shoot the guns or steer the ship. I would not be a cook.

On the wall of my classroom there's a map of the world hanging like a picture. Australia is yellow on the map. That's the last colour I think Australia is. Australia is not yellow. Not where I live it isn't, anyway. Yellow is the colour of happiness. Yellow is the colour of the sun. Yellow is the colour of balloons or flowers. Where I live is not yellow. Treymouth is not yellow. Treymouth is grey and grey is the colour of unhappiness, as far as I can see.

Outside it's raining. Again.

After school I walk back through town past the shops. Some shops are open, some shops are empty and shut - but one that was empty and shut this morning is now sort of open. There's someone in it, anyway. This shop used to sell presents like tea towels, china horses, and dolls, but it closed down months ago. I stop and look in the window, past my reflection. There's a tall man up a ladder fixing a light. He's put up quite a few lights. And there's a wooden wall standing where there wasn't one this morning, although it's not finished yet. It's a framework.

The man sees me and climbs down the ladder. He's wearing these coloured baggy pants and old red shoes. He walks to the door and opens it with a key that was already in the lock. I guess he's going to tell me to go away, but I don't move. He doesn't look angry. I would like to know what he's doing.

'Gidday, mate', he says, as if we already know each other. He looks out past me to the river. 'Geez, she's a rainy, rainy, chilly old day.'

He smiles above me, at the sky. 'I hope it doesn't rain like this every day.'

Mostly it does, but new people in town don't want to hear that.

'No, not every day,' I say. 'Sometimes in summer it's hot.' But summer feels a long way off. It's not even spring yet. Summer feels as if it's lost somewhere over the other side of the world.

I don't turn to look at the river. I look at the new wood and the tools lying on the shop floor. I can smell sawdust. It's good to look at something new being built. Mostly things around here just get old, fall down, and are never put back up again.

The man stops looking at the river. He takes a step back into the shop.

'This is going to be a pet shop,' he tells me and waves his hands around. ' A beautiful pet shop! Soon she'll be full of fish, lizards, birds. All sorts of things.' He smiles. I see a small black hole where one of his side teeth is missing. 'You like animals, don't you? I can tell you do.'

I nod. I do like animals.

On his coloured pants the man has pictures of fruit: slices of pink watermelon with black pips, yellow pineapples and stripy bananas. He's wearing a green jumper full of holes and has silver earrings in both his ears. He looks quite old with wrinkles on his face and hands, but he seems quite young. He has long straight pale hair and leathery gold skin. I can tell he's not from around here. He looks different. He looks like he might be from somewhere sunny or strange.

'I'm Clarence John Sellew,' he says, 'by the way.' He puts out his hand. 'Call me Clarence.'

I shake his hand. It's dry, creased like old newspaper, and warm. His eyes are bright and brown.

'I'm Davey Collins,' I say, and put my hand back in my pocket. I can still feel the warmth from Clarence's hand. 'There's never been a pet shop here before,' I tell him. 'I don't remember one, anyway. I'm glad there will be, though. It'll be good.'

Clarence seems happy to hear that. He lights half a cigarette that he takes out of a matchbox, and waves it at the stack of wood and the box of tools.

'And the pets will be here soon.' He smiles, showing the black tooth-hole again. 'Soon as the girls and my brother Ezra bring the stock over, there'll be birds, fish, snakes, lizards, pups - all sorts of things. You tell your friends.'

'I will,' I say, although I don't have many friends. Just kids at school. 'Bye.' I walk away from Clarence's door.

A new shop. A pet shop. Good! But there's always a new shop opening up, and hardly any of them last. After a while they have plenty of special bargains, then a Final Sale, then they're gone, and the letters and newspapers pile up for the snails.

I look around. It's still raining.

  2.  Because he thinks it is an embarrassing job.

  3.  Because he thinks that cooking is a job for women.

  4.  All of the above.
