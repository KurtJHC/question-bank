2     Fred is competing in the Iditasport, and it’s one tough race. It has been held every February for 14 years on the same trail as the world-famous Iditarod sled dog race in Alaska. But in this race competitors rely on their own strength, not that of dogs. They can run, snowshoe, ski, or ride bicycles over the rough terrain. Bicyclists and skiers travel the entire length of the course, which is 160 miles. Runners and snowshoers fly to the Skwentna checkpoint and race 85 miles to the finish line (see map). There are checkpoints for eating and resting along the way, but deep snow, steep hills, and frozen rivers and lakes also await racers. The record time for completing the race is 15 hours, 17 minutes, set by a bicyclist in 1993.

3     Only the hardiest athletes enter. Fred and his friend, Thad Fitzgerald, also 16 and from Anchorage, were the two youngest racers in 1996. It was Fred’s fourth Iditasport! He began competing when he was 13 years old. There is no minimum age to enter the race, but kids under 18 must have an adult’s permission.

4     Every competitor must carry a survival kit. It includes matches, stove, fuel, a pot (to melt snow for drinking water), cold-weather sleeping bag, tent, matches, insulated water bottle, headlamp, and at least one day’s supply of food. Fred’s supply included bagels and a bar of high-energy food. Thad scarfed down four boxes of toaster pastries.

I now worry that, just like many collegiate athletes, some high school players will begin neglecting their studies while pinning their hopes on a professional sports career. Some athletes with the ability to play at the college level may lose out on that chance because they focused on athletics rather than their schoolwork.

6     The NBA should stop the practice of drafting high school players and encourage them to develop both their academic and athletic skills. This would send a message to student athletes that their grade point average is just as important as their average points per game.

Letters to the Editor

 

1     Editor:

2    As a teacher and a coach, I feel sad when any high school student gives up the chance to go to college. The lure of professional sports with its big money, flashy lifestyle and adoring fans would be hard for anyone to turn down, much less a youngster who hasn’t even graduated. Offering these temptations to high school students is almost criminal.

3     Not too long ago students hoping to go to college participated in sports as a way of earning a scholarship—their opportunity at a college degree. Now, however, sports have become the focus and many athletes attend college only for the purpose of showcasing their athletic talent and getting an offer to play professionally. That’s too bad since the chances of playing professional sports are extremely small. Even for the talented few who do enter the professional ranks, injuries often result in “careers” that are very brief.

4     Unfortunately many college athletic programs fail to acknowledge this reality. Athletes often spend four years playing football or basketball for a college without managing to earn a degree. Many of these athletes realize too late that their athletic ability will carry them only so far. Injuries end the careers of some, but more often than not, the professional teams aren’t interested in them. Although some of these athletes do manage to earn college degrees, many others are left holding a shattered dream, wishing it was a diploma.

5     Now these same problems are moving down to the high school level. Some talented athletes are choosing to skip college altogether to play professionally in the National Basketball Association TM (NBA). Although a few, such as Kevin Garnett, have gone on to be stars, these players are the exception—not the rule. I now worry that, just like many collegiate athletes, some high school players will begin neglecting their studies while pinning their hopes on a professional sports career. Some athletes with the ability to play at the college level may lose out on that chance because they focused on athletics rather than their schoolwork.

6     The NBA should stop the practice of drafting high school players and encourage them to develop both their academic and athletic skills. This would send a message to student athletes that their grade point average is just as important as their average points per game.

7     Howard Evans

Thorns and Roses

by Margaret S. Frezon

 

Sarah sat up in bed and rubbed her eyes. Where were all of her old familiar things? The framed picture of the cocker spaniel puppy was not hanging on the wall opposite her bed. The navy blue blanket that had always covered the foot of her bed seemed to have gone missing in the night. The curtains had magically changed from sky-blue to sea-green. Everything had changed. “All part of my new life,” she thought, frowning.

When Sarah’s family had moved just a few weeks ago, everyone seemed happy. Their new house was located in a town next to a national forest, twenty miles closer to her mother’s job. There would be an extra bedroom in the new house for her great aunt, who had been living alone.

Despite her family’s enthusiasm, Sarah was having a hard time getting used to the changes. She missed her old school and she missed the friends who had lived on her block. She missed all the things that had been left behind, from the curtains to the colors of the walls. Since it was summer, there was no opportunity to make friends at school. What was she supposed to do all day?

Seeing Sarah moping around, her great aunt Felicia said, “You could always come out and help me get the garden whipped into shape.” Sarah was not exactly in the mood for gardening, but this offer was the only one she had. Besides, she loved her great aunt and wanted to be helpful. She shrugged and followed her into the yard.

Sarah had to agree that the garden needed a lot of work. The plot itself was fairly large but few flowers could be seen, and the overgrown greenery was choked with weeds. The little fence around the perimeter of the garden was old and wobbly. Sarah began to wonder if it was worth weeding the plot at all. With a little sigh, she knelt down and began yanking clumps of weeds.

“Ouch!”

Sarah yanked her hand back and stared at a crimson drop on the tip of her finger. “Why do rosebushes have to have thorns?” she complained aloud.

“Sorry, baby,” her great aunt called. “I should have brought us some gardening gloves. Working around roses can be prickly work.”

“That’s okay, Aunt Felicia,” Sarah replied. “I’ll survive.”

They worked through the afternoon and then decided to quit for the day.

 

The next day, Sarah woke up before anyone else and slipped outside to the garden. She was especially careful to avoid the thorns of the rosebush. As she pulled weeds and grass, she noticed a bud just beginning to blossom. It looked delicate. Just a trace of pink peeked out from under the green covering. Sarah knew that it would soon open into a full and fragrant blossom. And then she noticed another bud and another—the bush was filled with them. She could not help but let out a happy shout.

“I see you found the roses,” Aunt Felicia said from the back porch.

 

Aunt Felicia came down the back porch steps and put her arms around Sarah. “Suppose we take a walk around our new neighborhood,” she said. “I’ll bet we can find more roses or other things just as nice.”
