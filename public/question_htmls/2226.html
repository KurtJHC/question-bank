Why be different? By seeing that no other being has our special perspective on life, that no one else has exactly the same experiences that we do, and that no one else creates the same meanings and rules we do, we then can be confident about our own reality and let others create their own. To be different and to be comfortable in that perceived difference gives us the freedom to act from our core and be our most authentic selves. Without a sense that we are different and that ‘that’s a good thing’, we can’t trust the most subtle and profound messages coming from within. We don’t have to measure what we are or what we desire by any other standard than our own sense of what works for us and what doesn’t. So, I now make an effort to create my life from the perspective that this life of mine is like a painter’s palette and canvas. I can do this any way I choose, and my painting will be different from anyone else’s. That’s what makes it art.

A chameleon is a type of lizard that is born with the ability to change its colour depending on the need and can even adopt the colours of its environment. This gives it the advantage of protection, hunting for food and being accepted by its mates. In many ways, humans are like chameleons. As social animals, humans have a natural tendency to blend in with their environment and social groups to achieve a sense of acceptance, security, and love. People who are different and without groups to belong to, are vulnerable in many ways, as they can become susceptible to discrimination, marginalisation, bullying and have a weakened sense of identity. It is dangerous to be different to the rest of society.

Mandela made a difference to others because his vision was one of inclusion and hope. It was also one of reconciliation and civility in political debate. Above all, he believed in education: “Education is the most powerful weapon with which you can hope to change the world”, he said. He also considered love to be the best antidote to racism: “People must learn to hate, and if they can learn to hate, they can be taught to love, for love comes more naturally to the human heart than its opposite”. Finally, he taught us how to live: “The greatest glory in living lies not in never falling, but in rising every time we fall”, and “to be free is not merely to cast off one’s chains, but to live in a way that respects and enhances the freedom of others”. When he left politics, Mandela remained active in philanthropy, especially by fighting the scourge of AIDS in Africa (he actually lost his last son to AIDS). Mandela lived by example and spoke powerful words that truly made a difference in the lives of many.

Ableism is a set of beliefs or practices that devalue and discriminate against people with physical, intellectual, or psychiatric disabilities, and often rests on the assumption that disabled people need to be ‘fixed’ in one form or another. Ableism is deeply rooted in our  culture due to the many limiting beliefs about what disability does or does not mean, how able-bodied people learn to treat people with disabilities, and how they are often not included at the table for key decisions. The best way to de-root ableism in our everyday lives is to ensure that there’s always a seat at the table for those who are like you and those who are not, but also assess how we treat people with disabilities once they are at the table. As simple as this sounds, de-rooting ableism is often as simple as just treating disabled people like you would anyone else.
