The Red Fox

by Donna Stringfellow

It was cold in the forest. A bitter March wind rattled bare trees like skeletons and whipped up the dark clouds in an iron gray sky. The approaching snowstorm probably would not be the last one of the winter.

The red fox couldn’t have chosen a worse time to bring a litter of kits1 into the world. Nestled in a small hollow beneath a hickory tree, curled against their mother’s plush fur, the three young kits were warm and comfortable. But when the freezing storms came, the shallow nest would surely let in the snow. And it would be too easy for predators to find the babies when their mother left them to search for food. The fox knew she would have to seek a new home, and soon.

She nuzzled her kits, whose eyes were not yet open. She licked them, and they mewed about her like kittens. Then she left them. Outside her den, cold air stung her nose as she sniffed about for danger. Then she padded off into the gray, wintry forest.

She ducked beneath a wooden fence and followed a path across a familiar field, where during the summer she’d chased rabbits. She was near a farm, a place she’d always avoided because of the fearful smell of humans. But now, the warmth and protection of the barn drew her close.

Squeezing through the gap where a board was missing, she sneaked into the barn. The straw was deep and soft, a perfect bed for fox kits.

The red fox hurried back to her babies. One by one she carried them under the fence, across the field, and through the hole in the barn wall. And when all three kits were snuggled down in the blanket of straw, she licked them and felt safe. Even when the farmer came to milk his cows, the silent fox knew her family would be unnoticed, hidden in the farthest and darkest corner of the barn.

One snowy evening, the farmer turned off his lantern and hung it on the wall as he left the barn, just as he did every night. But as he closed the door, the lantern slipped from its hook and shattered on the floor. A tiny spark danced across the pool of kerosene2 and nibbled at the scattered straw.

The fox watched with wide, yellow eyes as the straw curled and caught flame. Wisps of smoke reached her nostrils, and she became fearful. Slipping through the hole in the wall, she ran outside and howled. Her voice carried across the barnyard, and the farmer turned around. He was surprised and angry to see a fox. He was even more angry to see it run into his barn.

Setting down his milk pail, the farmer headed back to his barn, determined to chase away the unwelcome visitor. But when he threw open the barn door, fire danced about his feet. Grabbing a shovel, he beat the flames until they were out. Once again, the barn was safe and dark.

Taking up a flashlight, the farmer shone its beam about the barn. It finally came to rest on the fox, her family nestled close, her eyes shining gold. The farmer smiled. He flicked off the light and walked away, quietly closing the barn door behind him.

Winter’s harshness gave way to spring’s gentle warmth. One day the farmer glanced into the darkest corner of the barn and found it empty. But for a time it had been a place to feel safe. It had been home to a red fox family.

As American as . . . Pizza Pie?
by Michael Demchsak

1     In America, the pizza industry makes more than 30 billion dollars a year. Americans eat 350 slices of pizza per second. A pizza that would satisfy the cravings of the American public each day would cover a 100-acre field. Among kids ages 3–11, pizza is reported to be the first choice for either lunch or dinner.

2     Consequently, it is not surprising that pizza seems ready to take the title of “America’s favorite food” away from the hamburger. 

3     The success achieved by the pizza pie is fairly amazing. After all, pizza was an ethnic food found only in big-city Italian neighborhoods until around 60 years ago. 

Ancient History 
4     In the area around the Mediterranean Sea, people have been baking flat breads, usually covered with herbs, for thousands of years. In fact, the Latin word picea refers to bread blackening in an oven. It may be the origin of the term pizza. The flat breads baked by these Mediterranean people, especially the Italians, were sometimes topped with cheese or vegetables. The result was fairly close to being pizza. But these culinary efforts were not what most of us think of when we hear the word pizza.

5     Two of the essential ingredients—the crust and the cheese—were in place. It is hard to imagine pizza without tomato sauce, but the tomato is not native to the Mediterranean. It would be many years before this third crucial ingredient would be available. The tomato is a New World plant. It would not have been available in Europe until the 16th century, when Spanish explorers brought it back from Mexico. 

More Recent History 
6     In the 16th and 17th centuries, the city of Naples in Italy began producing the ancestors of the tasty treats we now call pizzas. Vendors carried pizzas through the streets in metal stoves worn on their heads. This practice of taking the product to the customer helped increase the popularity of pizza. The first sit-down pizzeria, the Antica Pizzeria Port’Alba, opened in 1830. It continues to operate to this day.

7     It was not until 1889 that pizza had its great moment in history. A restaurant owner in Naples was asked to make a dish in honor of the visiting Queen Margherita. He decided to recreate a version of the Italian flag on crusty flat bread. Tomatoes, mozzarella cheese and a puree of basil formed the red, white and green of the Italian flag. The dish was a giant hit. In fact, visitors to Naples today can still see a royal thank you note from June 1889 written by Galli Camillo, the head of the table of the royal household of Italy.

8     Italian immigrants brought their love of pizza to the United States. It was popular in Italian neighborhoods in the early 20th century. The first pizzeria in the United States opened in 1905 in New York City. For a time, the popularity of pizza remained restricted mostly to cities where large groups of Italians lived. But servicemen who had been stationed in Italy during World War II (1941–1945) sparked a new enthusiasm for pizza as they returned home. That spark obviously turned into a wildfire. 

Getting Creative with Pizza 
9     Pizza is an incredibly versatile dish. Over time, modifications to pizza and its ingredients have become common. Purists can debate whether or not a particular combination is really pizza. But personal and cultural preferences guarantee that pizza will vary widely—and wildly— depending where in the world it is found. At the moment, pizza can be eaten in this country with regular hand-tossed, deep-dish, or ultra-thin crust. Seemingly unusual ingredients are not very unusual at all. For example, Americans with a taste for something different can often ask for a pizza topped with anything from pineapple to sauerkraut. In other lands, the choices may be even more interesting. There are squid pizzas in Japan and mackerel pizzas in Russia. There are coconut pizzas in Costa Rica and curry pizzas in Pakistan.

Pizza Today 
10     In America today, there are approximately 61,000 pizzerias. Pizza is so common that it is hard to imagine that there are people today who grew up in a time when many Americans had never heard of pizza or seen a slice of this food favorite. It seems almost sad. But those times are gone. Now it can be claimed that America is the pizza capital of the world. But . . . shhh! Don’t tell Naples.
