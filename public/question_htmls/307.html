Pluto

by James Trefil

 

    Pluto is in many ways the strangest of the planets. It is small and has a large moon (called Charon). Its orbit is eccentric, which may cause it to have seasons in the sense that when it is close to the sun, the liquid methane on its surface boils to form a kind of atmospheric haze. When the planet moves farther away from the sun, it starts to snow solid methane.

    Pluto is not dark. Despite its great distance from the sun, the surface of Pluto is probably as bright as a moonlit night on Earth. The reason is all that methane, which is as white as newly fallen snow.

    The discovery of Pluto was more accident than design. The American astronomer Percival Lowell had predicted the existence of a ninth planet (he called it Planet X) based on what he took to be irregularities in the orbit of Neptune. Today astronomers argue that these “irregularities” weren’t real, but the result of instrumental error. Nevertheless, Lowell produced predictions about where Planet X ought to be (although, to be honest, the predictions changed occasionally when he redid the calculations). In any case, in 1930 Clyde Tombaugh, doing a systemic sky survey that would have found the planet no matter where it was, discovered the planet we now call Pluto. By coincidence, its position was pretty close to where Lowell’s last prediction said it should be. Was it just luck? We’ll never know.

Text Two: Biography of Pemulwuy

1     Pemulwuy was an Aboriginal that lived in the Sydney area between the coast and Castle Hill in the Eora language group. He was born in about 1760, died in 1802 and saw the British as invaders of Aboriginal land.

2     Pemulwuy first came to the attention of the British when he speared and killed the governor's gamekeeper, John McIntyre, in 1790. McIntyre was believed to have killed a number of Aboriginal people in the area. Governor Phillip ordered a punitive expedition to revenge the death of the gamekeeper, but the troops failed to capture Pemulwuy. He was then declared an outlaw under British law.

3     Pemulwuy was responsible for organising small groups of Aboriginal warriors to attack British farms, small towns and troops around Parramatta and Toongabbie. Many settlers abandoned their properties as the raids continued.

4     Soldiers were soon ordered to patrol farming areas and protect the settlers. Pemulwuy and his warriors then began using fire as a weapon. They lit fires in the hope of destroying the British farms, fences, crops, stock, houses and supplies. The British responded by organising revenge attacks against the Eora people. The Eora camps were attacked while the men were away hunting. Elderly people, women and children were shot and either wounded or killed.

5     After several years of organising resistance against the settlers, Pemulwuy was shot and seriously wounded during an attack on Parramatta in 1797. He was captured and imprisoned. Despite his injuries, Pemulwuy somehow escaped. The Eora people believed he turned into a crow and flew through the bars of his prison cell.

6     For 12 years, Pemulwuy and his warriors fought against the British, although he found it increasingly difficult to find strong warriors to make the raids. Many Aboriginal people were dying from the European diseases and in battles with the settlers. In 1802 a patrol shot Pemulwuy dead in an ambush. His head was cut off and it was sent to Sir Joseph Banks in London for research and display in a British museum.

Pemulwuy’s initial action of killing the gamekeeper was most likely:
  1.  justified by British law.
  2.  revenge-killing.
  3.  jealousy.
  4.  an accident.

Text Three: Monster Waves


1    The word ‘tsunami’, Japanese for ‘harbour wave’, is used to describe a series of ocean surges that send waves of water, reaching on average between 10m to 30m in height, onto land. These awesome waves are typically caused by large, underwater earthquakes, which move the water above it and launch them into rolling waves that race towards land. At top speed, tsunamis can travel up to 805 km/hr – as fast as a jet airplane!

2    Tsunamis do not usually take the form of one large wave, as commonly shown in paintings. A tsunami is usually composed of a series of waves, called a wave train, which increases the tsunami’s destructive force as one wave after another reaches the shore. They also rarely appear as massive waves, but as an increasing tide that inundates areas by the shore, rapidly moving inland and washing away anything in its path, including buildings. Most tsunamis, about 80%, happen within the Pacific Ocean’s ‘Ring of Fire’, a geographical area where earthquakes and volcanic eruptions – another cause of tsunamis – are common.

3    There is a subcategory of tsunamis that are so large and destructive that they were given the name ‘mega-tsunamis’. These extreme waves tower over standard tsunamis, often reaching well over 100m in height. The biggest of these was recorded in Lituya Bay, Alaska, at 22:15 hours on July 9, 1958. A large earthquake, reading 7.9 out of 10 on the Richter Scale, had struck south eastern Alaska, immediately triggering a major subaerial landslide of 30 million cubic metres of rock and ice in Gilbert Inlet at the head of the bay. This power and disruption to the water generated a huge wave that peaked at 1720 ft, or 524 m – 143 m higher than the roof of the Empire State Building in New York, the world’s tallest building at the time.

4    Not surprisingly, the wave caused major damage to the surrounding landscape. Infrastructure such as bridges, docks and oil lines all sustained damage, a water tower collapsed, and a cabin was damaged beyond repair. Trees that were impacted by the wave were also uprooted, creating a continuous empty area near the shoreline. The area, was, however, largely unpopulated; at the time, there were three fishing boats anchored near the entrance of Lituya Bay, one of which sunk, resulting in the death of the two crewmen on board, and the other two riding the wave, leaving a number of eyewitnesses who later provided accounts of their shocking observations.

  1.  Drenches

  4.  Deluges

Text Three: Monster Waves


1    The word ‘tsunami’, Japanese for ‘harbour wave’, is used to describe a series of ocean surges that send waves of water, reaching on average between 10m to 30m in height, onto land. These awesome waves are typically caused by large, underwater earthquakes, which move the water above it and launch them into rolling waves that race towards land. At top speed, tsunamis can travel up to 805 km/hr – as fast as a jet airplane!

2    Tsunamis do not usually take the form of one large wave, as commonly shown in paintings. A tsunami is usually composed of a series of waves, called a wave train, which increases the tsunami’s destructive force as one wave after another reaches the shore. They also rarely appear as massive waves, but as an increasing tide that inundates areas by the shore, rapidly moving inland and washing away anything in its path, including buildings. Most tsunamis, about 80%, happen within the Pacific Ocean’s ‘Ring of Fire’, a geographical area where earthquakes and volcanic eruptions – another cause of tsunamis – are common.

3    There is a subcategory of tsunamis that are so large and destructive that they were given the name ‘mega-tsunamis’. These extreme waves tower over standard tsunamis, often reaching well over 100m in height. The biggest of these was recorded in Lituya Bay, Alaska, at 22:15 hours on July 9, 1958. A large earthquake, reading 7.9 out of 10 on the Richter Scale, had struck south eastern Alaska, immediately triggering a major subaerial landslide of 30 million cubic metres of rock and ice in Gilbert Inlet at the head of the bay. This power and disruption to the water generated a huge wave that peaked at 1720 ft, or 524 m – 143 m higher than the roof of the Empire State Building in New York, the world’s tallest building at the time.

4    Not surprisingly, the wave caused major damage to the surrounding landscape. Infrastructure such as bridges, docks and oil lines all sustained damage, a water tower collapsed, and a cabin was damaged beyond repair. Trees that were impacted by the wave were also uprooted, creating a continuous empty area near the shoreline. The area, was, however, largely unpopulated; at the time, there were three fishing boats anchored near the entrance of Lituya Bay, one of which sunk, resulting in the death of the two crewmen on board, and the other two riding the wave, leaving a number of eyewitnesses who later provided accounts of their shocking observations.
