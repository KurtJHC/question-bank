Ryan wrote this paper about some pets that belonged to U.S. presidents. Read his rough draft and think about how he can correct and improve it. When you finish reading, answer the questions that follow.

 


Presidential Pets 

 
     (1) George Washington was the first in a line of presidential animal lovers. (2) His most prized pet was his horse Nelson. (3) Other U.S. presidents have also had special animals in their family. (4) However, some of these White House pets were more unusual than others. 

     (5) Thomas Jefferson, the third president, kept some strange pets. (6) He had two bears, and they were gifts from the explorers who were Lewis and Clark. (7) He also had a mockingbird. (8) The state bird of Texas is the mockingbird. 

     (9) John Quincy Adams had an alligator, and James Buchanan kept an elephant. (10) Abraham Lincoln saved a White House turkey that was supposed to be cooked for a special dinner. (11) His son named it Jack and made it part of the household. 

     (12) The largest collection of unusual pets belongs to Calvin Coolidge, the 30th president. (13) He and First Lady Grace Coolidge loved animals. (14) They received many pets as gifts. (15) These included lions, a wallaby, and a pygmy hippo. (16) President Coolidge’s favrit pet was a raccoon named Rebecca. 

     (17) The Presidential Pet Museum is located near Washington, D.C. (18) It is a great place to learn more about our nation’s “first pets.” 

What is the BEST way to revise sentence 6?
 
  1.  He had two bears that were gifts from the explorers Lewis and Clark. 
  2.  He had two bears, and they were gifts. From the explorers Lewis and Clark. 
  3.  He had two bears they were gifts from the explorers Lewis and Clark.
  4.  He had two bears. That were gifts from the explorers Lewis and Clark.

Going Along for the Ride

 

  In Australia a mother kangaroo hops along with a tiny head peeking out of a pocket on her belly. It’s an amusing sight. This pocket is called a pouch. For a baby kangaroo, rides in the mother’s pouch aren’t just for fun. 

  A baby kangaroo is called a joey. At birth it looks like a baked red bean. It is hairless, and its back legs are just little bumps. It can’t hear or see because its ears and eyes haven’t developed yet. To stay alive, the joey must quickly find its way into its mother’s pouch. She helps by licking a smooth path in her fur for the baby to follow. The joey has an acute sense of smell. Using this excellent sense of smell and strong arms, it reaches its mother’s pouch in about three minutes and drops inside. There it finds the milk that will keep it alive and growing for the next several months. 

  When the joey is about eight months old, the mother kangaroo relaxes her pouch muscles. Then the pouch droops open, and the joey tumbles out. It begins to explore the world outside. After looking around for a while, it goes back into its hiding place. 

  The joey soon learns to hop like its mother. When there is danger, the frightened joey jumps into the pouch. Its mother retreats at a speed of 15 to 20 miles per hour. If the mother senses that she might be caught, she hides the joey in the bushes. Then she hops away, trying to keep the pursuing person or animal away from her young. When she is no longer being followed, she goes back to get her joey. 

  A one-year-old joey is too big to stay in the pouch. Because the mother kangaroo usually has a baby every year, her pouch doesn’t stay empty very long.
