Read the simplified version of the short story by Australian author Ben Walker, then answer the question as given below.

Beast Evolving

"Can we keep him under control?" I asked. "Look at him run!"

Like a pet, the fire was jumping over hills, bringing back sticks for us to burn, and drinking from puddles in the grass. He even found water in trees. In the city, we had never had a pet like this before.

We thought it would be as good as having a real pet. He would keep us warm when the cold winter came in June. We brought him home and locked him in a dark metal firebox. In the mornings and evenings, we would bolt the door and let him warm our house. His smoke would rise up the chimney and smear across the sky. We would sit near him on the couch and watch his tricks—dancing flames and little jumps for sticks. At night, he would curl around the logs until they turned into soft grey ash. Later, we would feed him old branches from the apple and plum trees and use him to cook meat in the warm months.

"Can we keep him from breaking loose?"

But he is always getting loose. If the kids leave the door or gate open, he runs out! The fire races over cars, burns feathers and fur, and scratches the ground with his coal-black claws. He picks up bunches of pine trees in bright flames. We see him racing from tree to tree, jumping from one house to another, burning roofs and peeling paint. As the years go by, he grows bigger and stronger. His chest swells with pride, and he grins a nasty grin while eating up dry leaves.

Is it ever possible to stop a fire from spreading?

We try to stop him. We splash water on him to put him out, but he only growls. We bury him in the yard, building a clay oven to trap him, but he breaks through the walls and runs again. We try special chemicals and heavy coats, but he keeps escaping, making sparks that spread across the whole state.

"Can we control the fire that run like a wild beast?"

Now, the footpaths melt like old frost. The lawns are brown and dead. Kids play in dust, and we water our tomatoes with drops of sweat. Our city is like a cracked boiled egg, ready to break with just a tap. We search the river, hoping to find a fish. Some people live beside it now. Every winter, we burn the mountain, hoping it will stop the bigger fires. The harbour has widened, but when the fires come, they smash our homes and gardens. We remember the years when suburbs burned down and were slowly rebuilt. There are signs remembering places like Lenah Valley and Sandy Bay. But we don't get too attached anymore. Every year, new suburbs rise like birds coming back to life. They greet us in their bright voices: "Hello cocky, hope you like it here!"

We bury him in the yard, dig a kiln of clay to trap him, but he shatters the walls and runs again.

The whole city is now our hearth and home. We live in fear. The little lion cub who once played with us has grown into a huge wild beast. He lives deep in the bush now, but he keeps coming back. The fire keeps coming back. Thick smoke rolls over the city like new clouds, making the sun look red and making our eyes sting. We cough and struggle to find our homes, our families, our safety, through the brown and white smoke. The trees that burn lift up into the sky, and the crazy weather keeps getting worse.

"Can we afford to keep him any longer?"

When the narrator asks, “Can we keep him”, who is the “him”?
 

  1. A dog

  2. A dragon

  3. The fire

  4. A lion

The Bush Fire (1905) 

Ah, better the thud of the deadly gun, and the crash of the bursting shell,
Than the terrible silence where drought is fought out there in the western hell;
And better the rattle of rifles near, or the thunder on deck at sea,
Than the sound — most hellish of all to hear — of a fire where it should not be.

On the runs to the west of the Dingo Scrubs there was drought, and ruin, and death,
And the sandstorm came from the dread north-east with the blast of a furnace-breath;
Till at last one day, at the fierce sunrise, a boundary-rider woke,
And saw, in the place of the distant haze, a curtain of light blue smoke.

There is saddling-up by the cockey's hut, and out in the station yard,
And away to the north, north-east, north-west, the bushmen are riding hard.
The pickets are out and many a scout, and many a mulga wire,
While Bill and Jim, with their faces grim, are riding to meet the fire.

It roars for days in the hopeless scrubs, and across, where the ground seems bare,
With a cackle and hiss, like the hissing of snakes, the fire is travelling there;
Till at last, exhausted by sleeplessness, and the terrible toil and heat,
The squatter is crying, 'My God! the wool!' and the farmer, 'My God! the wheat!'

A bushfire is an unplanned vegetation fire. It is a generic term that includes grass fires, forest fires and scrub fires. Bushfires are a natural, essential, and complex part of the Australian environment and have been for thousands of years. Bushfires can be started by natural causes, such as lightning strikes, or by people (accidentally or on purpose). Weather and fuel conditions both affect whether a bushfire will start. Materials such as leaf litter, bark, small branches and twigs, grasses and shrubs can provide fuel for bushfires. Dry fuel is more likely to catch fire and burn easily; damp or wet fuel may not burn. The type of fuel that is available to burn, how much of it there is, and how dry or moist it is will influence bushfire conditions. Hot, dry and windy weather can increase the risk and severity of fire danger.
