Old Man Platypus 

 

Far from the trouble and toil of town, 

Where the reed beds sweep and shiver, 

Look at a fragment of velvet brown - 

Old Man Platypus drifting down, 

Drifting along the river. 

 

And he plays and dives in the river bends 

In a style that is most elusive; 

With few relations and fewer friends, 

For Old Man Platypus descends 

From a family most exclusive. 

 

He shares his burrow beneath the bank 
With his wife and his son and daughter 

At the roots of the reeds and the grasses rank; 

And the bubbles show where our hero sank 

To its entrance under water. 

 

Safe in their burrow below the falls

They live in a world of wonder,

Where no one visits and no one calls,

They sleep like little brown billiard balls

With their beaks tucked neatly under. 

 

And he talks in a deep unfriendly growl

As he goes on his journey lonely;

For he's no relation to fish nor fowl,

Nor to bird nor beast, nor to horned owl;

In fact, he's the one and only!

Read the four extracts below on the theme of the Moon. 

For questions 1 – 10, choose the option (A, B, C or D) which you think best answers the question. 

Which extract… 

confirms that there is no permanent ‘dark side’ of the Moon? 
 

  1. Extract A  

    The only total lunar (moon) eclipse of 2021 occurs this week and for families looking to enjoy the “Super Flower Blood Moon” eclipse, here’s everything you need to know. 

     

    On May 26, there will be a total lunar eclipse. During a lunar eclipse, the Moon travels through Earth’s shadow as our planet is directly in between the sun and our moon. In a total lunar eclipse, the Moon is completely covered by the Earth’s shadow, while a partial lunar eclipse occurs when only a part of the Moon moves into Earth’s shadow. This eclipse will occur at the same time as a “super-moon,” an informal term that describes times when the full moon is at its closest point to Earth, making it look extra big and bright in the night sky. Depending on where you live, you will need to wake up early (America) or stay up late (Australia) to see the eclipse. 

  2. Extract B  

    The Moon, so bright and large in the sky compared to other celestial objects, has captured the attention of humans at least since ancient times. Over these eras, mankind’s view of the Moon has changed, from the more mystical image of it as a god, to the thought it was covered in seas and vegetation. Most recently, it’s been viewed as a dry and dusty wasteland. Recent findings of water on the lunar surface could change the way we see our orbiting companion. 

     

    The Moon has even been blamed for some of the darker forces of human nature, such as temporary insanity. The term lunatic (and “loony”) comes from the Latin name for the Moon. Many criminal and insane behaviours were once blamed on the presence of a full moon. The full moon was also thought to change afflicted humans into fearsome werewolves, a mythological creature most common in European tales. Full moons and lunar eclipses were also seen by some cultures as bad omens.

  3. Extract C  

    If you have looked into the night sky, you may have noticed the Moon appears to change shape each night. The different shapes of the Moon that we see are called the Moon’s phases. 

     

    Why does this happen? The shape of the Moon isn’t changing throughout the month. But our view of the Moon does change. The Moon does not produce its own light. There is only one source of light in our solar system, and that is the Sun. “Moonlight” is actually just sunlight reflecting off of the Moon’s surface. The Sun’s light comes from one direction, and it always illuminates, or lights up, one half of the Moon – the side of the Moon that is facing the Sun at that time. The other side of the Moon is dark. On Earth, our view of the shiny part of the Moon changes each night, depending on where the Moon is in its orbit around Earth. When we have a full view of the completely illuminated side of the Moon, that phase is known as a full moon. But following that, we start to see less of the Moon lit by the Sun each night. Eventually, the Moon reaches a point in its orbit when we don’t see any of the Moon illuminated. This phase is called a new moon.

  4. Extract D  

    In 1504 the famous explorer, Christopher Columbus had been stranded with his men on the island of Jamaica. Columbus awaited rescue, but their food had run out, and the Jamaican tribesmen, who had been pleased to provision them when they first arrived, were tired of it. 

     

    Luckily Columbus had astronomical tables with him, which indicated a lunar eclipse was due on February 29. Calling the local chiefs together, Columbus unscrupulously tricked them by telling them that his God was all-powerful, and very displeased with the Jamaicans’ refusal to keep them fed, and as a sign of his anger the Moon would be darkened and turned the colour of blood that evening. Many of the natives laughed, but all were convinced when the eclipse began as Columbus had told them it would. To add to the effect, Columbus retired to his cabin, to consult with God and ask Him to withdraw His sign. In fact, he was timing the eclipse, re-emerging at the appropriate time. Convinced of the power of this God, the Jamaicans fell to their knees begging forgiveness. The stranded Europeans did not need anything again before their rescue six months later.
