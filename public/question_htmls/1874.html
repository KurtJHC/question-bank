By Edgar Guest 

When things go wrong, as they sometimes will,
When the road you’re trudging seems all up hill,

The silver tint of the clouds of doubt,
And you never can tell how close you are,
It may be near when it seems afar;
So stick to the fight when you’re hardest hit—
It’s when things seem worst that you mustn’t quit.

{^ Extract B ^}
Lance Hill's clothes hoist became a symbol of Australian domestic life in the 1950s but he did not invent it. Hill's design was based on a similar device patented by Adelaide man Gilbert Toyne in 1926. Hill's first batch of hoists was made with tubing salvaged from the frame of the underwater boom that had hung under the Sydney Harbour Bridge to catch enemy submarines during World War II. He designed a cast aluminium winding gear to hoist the line up into the breeze - and the Hill's hoist was born.

{^ Extract C ^}
The Australian man whose revolutionary invention made air travel safer for millions of people is being hailed an unassuming genius on the 100th anniversary of his birth. For plane passengers across the globe today, the inflatable escape slide - highlighted on in-flight safety cards and by cabin crew briefings before take-off - is a familiar part of flying. But much less well known is John Grant, the Australian inventor of this life-saving safety device. During the 1950s and early 1960s, planes had life rafts stored above seated passengers, which could be taken down and launched through the door in the event of a crash on water. But the process proved slow and cumbersome, making it more difficult during an emergency water landing.

​In 1965, Mr Grant, who hailed from Sydney, hit on the concept of combining the escape slide and the life raft into one inflatable device. Rather than using fixed stairs and separate inflatable life rafts, Grant envisaged an inflatable slide that could be launched during an emergency landing on water, enabling people to slide down, stay on the slide, release it from the aircraft, and float away from the plane unharmed.

POOR PANDORA

By Elizabeth Williams

I must warn you before I get into this story
Be prepared – its horrific, surprising and gory!
It’s made me so incredibly furious
Cause it’s an insult to woman and all who are curious
But hang on, I’m getting ahead of myself
Let’s jump back a few thousand years…

When a Greek man named Hesiod roamed the earth
And wanted to explain humanity’s birth
He wrote an epic poem – about a billion pages
About Prometheus, Pandora and the Myth of Five Ages. 
It’s complex – a myriad of lessons and teachings
But the first known mention of Pandora. 

Now, I know backstory can be tedious and boring
And the last thing I want is for you to be snoring
But there’s a few things you just have to know about
In order for the story to have any clout.
It all starts with Zeus, the boss-god of mythology
Who is actually not very nice. 

But he does give two brothers a chance to find
A way to make the ‘man’ in ‘mankind’.
So, Prometheus creates a whole host of boys
And Epimetheus gives them animal toys.
Out of care for his creation, Prometheus’ desire
Was to give his golden boys lashings of fire.
Zeus didn’t want mere mortals to have it
But Prometheus gave it anyway.

This caused Zeus to lose his… head
and chain him to a cliff where an eagle fed
on his liver, which grew back every day
only to appear, then again be hacked away.
Then Heracles rocks up and sets him free
Which makes Zeus rather irate.

So, Zeus thinks – ‘how can I make him suffer?
I know! What punishment could be tougher
Than to send him the gift of a girl?
So, he got Hephaestus to give it a whirl
Can you believe it? The first woman was made
As a punishment for men!

So, Pandora was fashioned by the hands of the gods
Who bestowed her with gifts – some of them odd - 
Her name means, ‘all gifts’ (she was given a lot!)
But most of all - she was crazy hot!
She was made to be irresistible, Aphrodite’s clone
But also, idle and full of deceit. 

But before on mankind, she was let loose,
She got an extra special gift especially from Zeus.
It was an enticing box, well, technically a jar
(‘box’ was a mistranslation most bizarre)
But here’s the catch – she was told never to open it.
But to leave it on the shelf.

So, of course she opens it – what else could she do?
I’m sure if it was in front of you, you’d open it too!
But when she does all hell breaks loose, quite literally!
And evil is unleashed on the earth to roam free – 
So, she shuts it quickly and all that remains 
Inside the jar is hope. 

Well, that’s according to the original myth
Over the years it’s been changed and messed with
But the essential story remains the same
The first woman, Pandora – gods’ little game –
But before I object, protest and wail
I must remember, it’s just a mythical tale.
