High Flight 

Oh! I have slipped the surly bonds of Earth
And danced the skies on laughter-silvered wings;
Sunward I’ve climbed, and joined the tumbling mirth

Up, up the long, delirious burning blue
I’ve topped the wind-swept heights with easy grace
Where never lark, or ever eagle flew –
And, while with silent, lifting mind I’ve trod
The high untrespassed sanctity of space,
Put out my hand, and touched the face of God.

Dolphins and Porpoises 

Dolphins and porpoises delight us with their playful antics and warm our hearts with their friendly faces. Marine species are the most well-known, but there are several species that live in freshwater rivers. All are air-breathing, warm-blooded mammals that nurse their young. 15 ………. Dolphins have longer snouts, bigger mouths, more curved dorsal fins, and longer, leaner bodies than porpoises. 

People use the terms dolphins, porpoises, and whales to describe marine mammals belonging to the order Cetacea (from the Greek work ketos, “large sea creature”). 16 …… The orca, or killer whale, for example, is actually the largest member of the dolphin family. Dolphins and porpoises are at the top of the food chain and have an important role in the overall balance of the marine environment. They can tell us a lot about the health of the ocean. This includes the presence of pollution or the decline in fish. They are also very popular with tourists. 

Dolphins are by far more prevalent than porpoises. Most scientists agree that there are 32 dolphin species (plus five closely related species of river dolphin) and only six porpoise species. The harbour porpoise, for example, has small, pointed flippers and no beak. The dorsal fin is small and triangular-shaped. 17 ………. Both have large, complex brains and a structure in their foreheads, called the melon. With this they generate sonar (sound waves) to navigate their underwater world.

18 ………. Dolphins and porpoises differ in their faces, fins, and body shapes. Dolphins have longer noses, bigger mouths, more curved dorsal fins, and longer, leaner bodies than porpoises. The Northeastern Spotted Dolphin has a falcate, or sickle-shaped dorsal fin and light spotting on the belly.

Other differences also include their faces (who can forget Flipper’s famous “grin”?), their fins, and their figures. Dolphins tend to have prominent, elongated “beaks”, while porpoises have smaller mouths. But the central distinguishing feature is actually their teeth. Dolphins all have cone-shaped teeth, whereas porpoises have flat, spade- shaped teeth. While this distinction may be apparent on a skull in a museum, it’s a tricky one to make in the field. 19 ………. The dolphin’s hooked or curved dorsal fin (the one in the middle of the animal’s back) also differs from the porpoise’s triangular dorsal fin. Generally speaking, dolphin bodies are leaner, and porpoises’ bodies are portly.

Dolphins are also more talkative than porpoises. Dolphins make whistling sounds through their blowholes to communicate with one another underwater. Scientists are pretty sure that porpoises do not do this. 20 ………. It is likely that more (or fewer) differences between dolphins and porpoises will be revealed as researchers continue to investigate these intriguing sentinels of the sea.
