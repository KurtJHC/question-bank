The Redback Leftovers

By Debra Oswald

 

No sane person could describe the Redback Bay Under 13 Leftovers as a ‘team’ really. Imagine the worst soccer team you ever saw and then triple it. Let me review the lineup of players to give you some idea.

Shane Borman (the fridge with a head) was a pretty good player - except for one problem. He kept growling and thumping everyone else on the field. Even players on his own team.

Someone’d bump Shane accidentally and he’d aim his fist. ‘Want me to thump ya? Do ya? I will. I’ll thump ya,’ he’d roar. And you had no trouble believing him.

Robbie Oldroyd copped quite a few of Shane’s threats. Something about him particularly riles Shane up. Robbie would just dance off, cackling.

Robbie went to my school, so I already knew he wasn’t on the same planet as other people. For a start, he was always singing to himself. In the middle of a soccer game he’d lose interest, wander off and swing back and forth on the goalposts or beat out a rhythm on the crossbar like it was a set of drums. Robbie made me laugh and he was never nasty - but I can see why he drove a lot of people crazy.

Mozzie was so small and scrawny, you’d pick him for maybe six years old. It was hard to believe he was nearly thirteen. You had to watch where you were going so you didn’t tread on tiny Mozzie.

That little guy was certainly fast, with decent ball skills. When I first saw him streak ahead of the pack, dribbling the ball, I thought - yes, one good player. But two seconds later, Mozzie got tackled and pulverised by one of the bigger players. He lay on the ground, too scared to get up, so small you could hardly see him above the stalks of grass.

There was a beefy girl called Sarah. Seriously tall, huge shoulders, legs like tree trunks. She was pretty slow and lumbering on the field. Sarah ended up in the Leftovers because she’d quit the girls’ team. I think she was sick of getting teased about being big and all that.

Anthony Zammit had been a hopeless player when he was five years old. But now he was thirteen, he was even worse.

Anthony’s speciality was the ‘air-kick’ where he’d swipe at the ball but only connect with the air and end up landing on his bum. But the thing about Anthony was that he didn’t care. He kept on hauling himself to his feet, still grinning. He was having a fantastic time, no matter how lousy he played. Anthony Zammit loved soccer more than any kid I ever met.

And Spencer - well, I hate to say it but Spencer was hopeless. Spencer kept trying these tricky manoeuvres but he’d always trip on his own feet and collapse face first in the dirt. Then Shane would run right over the top of him, grinding his big boot into Spencer’s back.

We had one player, Oliver, who didn’t even bother running after the ball. He just trudged grimly across the field, muttering to himself. Oliver had never played soccer in his life. He was only there because he’s done a deal with his mum to give one sport a go for a season.

At least Oliver turned up. Holly hardly ever showed. She always came running in late to training, panting and lopsided - with one soccer boot on, the other boot flapping in her hand. One side of her hair in a plait, the rest blowing loose. Too flustered and panicky to concentrate on soccer.

Holly had wanted to be in the girls’ team but she was too disorganised to register in time. That’s how she’d ended up a Leftover.

Okay, so we did have a few good players. Apart from Stefan, we had Maria. She had speed, brilliant ball skills and fierce determination. Maria’s old club never gave girls a decent chance to make the First Grade. She got so mad she switched clubs. Now Maria was out to prove something. Trouble was, she got stuck in the Leftovers.

How do Maria and Oliver differ?

  1. One is really tall and the other is really short.

  2. One is always late for training but the other is not.

  3. Maria is talented but Oliver is not.

  4. Oliver is rough and Maria is placid.

Building a Worm Farm

Presenter: Jane Edmanson [20/01/2007]

 

Jane explains the benefits of worm farms

There can be no doubt that worms are the best thing for your garden. Not ordinary worms, but true composting worms - red worms from the Amazon and Tiger Worms. Their job is to live in a worm farm and decompose organic matter to produce marvellous stuff called worm castings, which gives the garden a surge.

It is easy to get a worm farm established. It’s made up of levels of plastic containers. Start with a solid base, and then add others with perforation. The aim is to build layers for the worms to move through. Look for a base that collects the liquid, (which will drain from the top), and a tap at the bottom means it’s possible to collect the liquid in a bottle. It is absolutely fantastic for watering all your pot plants but it needs to be diluted at 10 to 1.

When setting up a worm farm firstly consider where to put it. A cool, dry space is fine, but not in the hot afternoon sun. Next step is to set up the base, or bedding area, which needs to be put down with a layer of moistened newspaper.

The worms are available in bulk from hardware stores or a general nursery. About 500 come in a little bag with some bedding material, which is usually sawdust. Just spread that out.

Only feed a little bit at this stage because they are getting settled into the bedding material. Use lettuce, old bits of banana, anything that you've got that's wasteful in the kitchen sink. Then add just enough newspaper or hessian over the top to keep them moist. Always make sure that the newspaper, or hessian, is kept moist. They will start to feed and after about two weeks, add the next layer on top and that's the time to start feeding all organic waste and the worms will travel up through the perforations.

Worms like moistened cardboard or newspaper, food scraps, and they love eggshells, but crunch them up a little. It gives them variety in their diet. Coffee grindings and tea leaves are absolutely fantastic and throw in all the kitchen scraps. But don’t add too much citrus and onions, which are acidic and worms don't like them that much. Just use a few.

Things to avoid include tough garden clippings because it takes a long time for a worm to chew through them and meat, because that attracts vermin. But dog hair and vacuum cleaning bags are fantastic. Then put the lid on to seal it.

If the worm farm is too dry and has ants, then add vegetable matter that rots down and some water to ensure it isn't too dry. Little vinegar flies hovering means it's too acidic, so add some lime and ruffle it up a little.

After four or five months the worm casts or vermicast on the bottom level will be ready to use. It’s powerful stuff. To get the worms out before putting the vermicast onto the garden, just put them in the sunlight and scrape the top layer of the worm castings off. The worms will then tunnel down to get away from the sunlight. Use vermicast wisely because it is potent. A piece about 10cm is enough to put around a shrub or pot plant. Just sprinkle it around plants and they will love it.

Many people think worms are yucky, but in fact they are quite clean. They have no known diseases and their digestive system destroys pathogens. The job they do cannot be compared to anything else. If everyone had a worm farm our compostable garbage would be reduced by 1 tonne per person per year. That's an amazing amount and worms do it all for you. They are really fantastic - so happy worm farming.

The liquid from a worm farm

  1. needs to be diluted or it is toxic to plants.

  2. should be put with ten times as much water.

  3. is healthy to drink.

  4. should be mixed with one cup of water.
