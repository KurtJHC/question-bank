{^ Extract A ^}
Christmas is my favourite time of the year. However, recently I’ve been finding the frenzy of shopping and wrapping gifts very stressful. Worse, I’ve begun to notice my children lose their passion and excitement after opening their 10th gift and I’m left alone under the Christmas tree, surrounded by wrapping paper and ribbons, to finish the task. At such moments I feel Christmas has become far too commercial.

{^ Extract B ^}
Christmas is a time to celebrate the birth of Jesus Christ. It’s a time of God showing his great love for us. It’s also a time for us to be with family and show our love. I’m not saying we should abandon the gift-giving tradition. But Christmas could be less about finding the right present and be as simple as cooking a festive meal for the family, or even making handmade paper snowflakes with the kids. In my group of friends, we no longer exchange Christmas gifts. My kids are allowed to keep five gifts and we donate the rest to orphanages and other charities. I want to teach them to be grateful for what they have and to show compassion to the less privileged. This is the true meaning of Christmas.

{^ Extract C ^}
Many have argued Christmas has become too commercial—just a time for toy, card and lighting companies to boost sales. To them I say: “No!”, Christmas remains the season of joy and love. There is nothing more enjoyable than finding the perfect gift for my loved ones. The very quest for these presents encapsulates the caring spirit of Christmas, for these gifts represent my love and thoughts. Nothing fills my heart with more joy than gifting that perfect present to a happy child, and nothing breaks my heart more than a parent who gives those gifts away or tries to teach their kids kindness by depriving them. What is the point? Let them enjoy Christmas and teach them to be charitable all year round and generous by nature. It does not need to become a competition at Christmas about who is least selfish or entitled.

{^ Extract D ^}
My family’s tradition is the perfect example of how the meaning of Christmas is alive and well. As a boy, my son would always ask me to deposit his red money packets from Chinese New Year. One year he asked to keep the money, which I allowed, and he kept it in a small box, which I checked periodically. One day in December I discovered it was gone. When I questioned him, he pulled out a small box containing a tiny charm bracelet that he’d spent the money on and wished me a Merry Christmas. I’ll never forget his huge smile and I’ve kept that bracelet to this day. So, to the cynics out there, stop worrying about the “meaning” of Christmas and just give a little something—for it is in the giving that we share our love and show that we care.
