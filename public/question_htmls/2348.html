“Shoot it, man, shoot it!”

“Na, na, Andrew. It doesna look like one o’ they devils. Look, it pays no heed to aught. Let’s see if it gangs near the sheep. For it seems to be paying no heed to them at all. Look.” 

“It’s a stray. We have a richt to shoot it!”

“Let’s see if it gangs near the sheep. If it does . . .”

“Och, ye gormless! Shoot it!”

The older man’s voice rose in urgent tones. The cry floated over the night to where Lassie trotted. She paused in her tracks and turned her head. Then it all struck her together—the sound of men, the scent of them, the movement in the window of the stone hut. It was man—man that would chain her, man that she must avoid.
She wheeled and sprang away in a sudden lope.

“There! It’s seen us! Let him have it!”

The sudden dash of Lassie half convinced the younger man that he had misjudged the dog below. For Lassie’s actions were like those of a guilty dog. He lifted the rifle quickly, cuddled the stock, and fired.

At the crack that shattered the night, Lassie leaped away. The ugly whine of a bullet passing by her left shoulder made her veer quickly to the right. She raced across the field. There was another shot, and she felt a burning shock in her flank.

“Nay, I hit it.”

“Ye didna. Look at it go!”

Inside the small shelter the voices of the men mixed with those of the dogs, who now cried pandemonium.

“Let ’em out!”

The old man raced to the door and opened it.

The dogs, then the men, tumbled out, and raced away after Lassie’s tracks.

“Go get it! Sic ’em!” yelled Andrew.

The dogs raced along, baying at the chase.

They went down the slope, bellies flat and their bodies almost doubling in two with the urgency of their speed. Behind them came the men, but they were soon left behind. The dogs suddenly swerved and bayed louder—for they had picked up the trail—the warm scent of new blood.

Ahead of them Lassie galloped. Twice she halted suddenly and snapped at the flank where the bullet had creased her leg muscles. She could hear the pursuing dogs behind, but she did not increase her pace. She had no fear of dogs. It was man she wished to leave behind, and her senses told her they were not near. But now she feared him more than ever. Not only could his hands chain and pen one up, but he could make the terrifying thunder noises that hurt the ears and that somehow reached out like a long, invisible whip and brought pain such as that which now tore at her.

Truly man was an evil menace.

  4. He feels sorry for the dog and tells the other man to stop.

PROGRESS

Strangely twisted
warped
but tender beauty
old grey-eyed soft
hollow spaces
hiding secrets for me
In your limbs
strong enough to hold
enfold and hide
way up inside
your wide high places
safe and carefree
I watched the faces pass
beneath — always hurrying!
Rarely seen by bigger busier
human beings
I stole small moments
from their lives
gave them names and made-up
places where they could live
inside my games
The indestructible other-world
— but they cut you down
for being unsound
fed you to a chip-machine
and widened the road
where you had been!

Heather Farmer

Solids, Liquids, and Gases

Matter is all around us – it is anything that takes up space and has mass. Everything we can see, touch, or feel is made of matter, from the air we breathe to the ground we walk on. Matter usually exists in one of three main forms, known as states of matter: solid, liquid, or gas. Each state has unique characteristics, but they are all made of tiny particles. Understanding these states helps us see how materials behave and change in our world.

(A) …………………… Solids hold their own shape and do not flow like water does. If you put a solid object into a container, it won’t change its form to fill the container – for example, a rock or a toy keeps the same shape wherever you put it. This is because the particles in a solid are packed tightly together and locked in place. Around you, many things are solids: the chair or desk you are using, the books on a shelf, and even the ice in a freezer are all solids that retain their shape.

A liquid is a state of matter that has a definite volume but no fixed shape. Unlike a solid, a liquid will flow and take the shape of the container you put it in. For example, if you pour water from a cup into a bowl, the water will spread out and assume the bowl’s shape. However, the amount of water (volume) stays the same – it doesn’t disappear or expand magically. (B) …………………… Similar to solids, liquids are very difficult to compress because their particles are still fairly close together. Common liquids include water, milk, and oil. At room temperature, for instance, water is a liquid that we can pour and drink.

A gas is a state of matter that has no fixed shape and no fixed volume. Gases will expand or spread out to fill the entire space available to them. If you release a gas into a container, it will quickly fill the container no matter the container’s size. (C) …………………… The air around us is a mixture of gases, including oxygen and nitrogen, which are crucial for life. Every time you breathe in, you are filling your lungs with these gases. Examples of gases you might know are the air in a balloon (often filled with helium gas) and water vapour (the steam you see when water boils).

Matter can change from one state to another when the conditions change, especially temperature. For example, when a solid is heated and turns into a liquid, the process is called melting. A familiar case is ice melting into liquid water. (D)…………………… If a liquid gains enough heat, it can turn into a gas through evaporation or boiling. In some special cases, a substance can go directly from solid to gas without becoming liquid in between – this is called sublimation. The opposite process, gas turning directly into a solid, is called deposition.

Solids, liquids, and gases are all around us and play an important role in our daily lives and the environment. Solids provide structure and support buildings and tools. Liquids like water are essential for life, cleaning, and transport. Gases are vital for breathing and are part of Earth’s atmosphere. (E) …………………… The planet’s water cycle is a great example of how all three states work together, as water evaporates into vapour, condenses into clouds, and falls as rain or snow. (F) …………………… By understanding these basic states of matter, we can better understand the natural world and create useful technologies for the future.
