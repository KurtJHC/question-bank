The story of the sad elephants is a true story that still has no explanation. It just happened and we still have no idea why.

A man named Anthony Lawrence was given a herd of elephants that nobody wanted because they were so wild. Nana, the matriarch of the herd continually tried to tear down the fence around the reserve. According to the story of the sad elephants, Lawrence would stand in front of them and explain the dangers they would face if they left the reserve. At first, Nana became curious but later that curiosity gave way to love. As a result, they stopped being a herd of rebellious elephants and became great friends with their caretaker, Anthony. 

In 2012, Anthony Lawrence died from a heart attack. Thus began a most amazing event. Two days after his death, the wild elephants began to arrive at his home. They were all there within a day. A total of 31 elephants walked more than 20 kilometres to get there. They surrounded the house, and stayed there for two days, without eating or drinking during that time. Maybe this was their way of saying goodbye to their great friend. On the third day, they left with the same solemnity with which they had arrived.

Nobody can explain how they found out about Anthonys death.

Modoc: The True Story of the Greatest Elephant That Ever Lived.
A captivating true story of loyalty, friendship, and high adventure that spans several decades and three continents, Modoc is one of the most remarkable true stories ever told, perfect for fans of The Zookeeper's Wife or Water for Elephants.

The more I read about the massive elephant brain, the more convinced I've become, as Graeme Shannon of Sussex University says, "there seems to be something going on there". He was talking about elephants in Kenya being able to distinguish between different languages English, safe, the language of tourists clicking cameras; Maa potentially dangerous, the language of the Maasai warriors who occasionally kill elephants; and Swahili, generally safe. The elephants seemed anxious when someone spoke Maa; the moment she switched to Swahili, they became calm.

Animal psychologist Karen McComb, also at Sussex, played back elephant sounds to discover how many individual voices one animal could recognise. The answer? More than 100. Research in Japan suggests they can count, too.

But it is the empathy of elephants that stands out, that makes them seem so alike to humans. "What elephants share with humans is that they live in an elaborate and complex network in which support, empathy, and help for others are critical for survival." Prof. Byrne.

{^ Extract D ^}
If you want to survive an elephant attack, firstly, look at its ears. If the elephant's ears are pinned back flat, it's about to charge you for real. If an elephant's ears are relaxed, it is probably making a mock charge. Ears that are fanned out are likewise indicative of a mock charge. 

Most charges made by elephants are mock charges. That is, the elephant is pretending to charge but is actually testing you out to see if you're aggressive or a non-threat. Look at the elephant's trunk to see if it's curled inward in attack position. During a genuine charge, an elephant will curl its trunk up and inward. If its trunk is hanging loosely down, on the other hand, the elephant is probably only making a mock charge.

Being able to recognise the difference between an elephant who is making a mock charge and an elephant who is about to trample you may save your life. Start running in a zig-zag pattern if you've noticed the signs of a real charge. A charging elephant can run much faster than you, but if you zig-zag, you might be able to evade it.
