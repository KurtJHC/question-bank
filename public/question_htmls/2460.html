From the Wave
by Thom Gunn

It mounts at sea, a concave wall
Down-ribbed with shine,
And pushes forward, building tall
Its steep incline.

Then from its summit into space
It rears and curls,
Descending with exulting pace
To sea-level.

Half-way, in air, there comes a cry
And on the wave
Two bodies balance, face to sky,
Alive, and brave.

The marbling bodies have become
Half wave, half men,
Grafted it seems by feet of foam
Some seconds, then,

Late as they can, they slice, and drive
Their heels in sand,
Re-forming the wave’s crested curve
Close to the land.

Balance is triumph in this place,
Triumph possession,
The possessor in tight-rope poise
With a sure foot’s concession,

In flight above the flowing curve,
With hair like kelp
Loose in the tide, a foot poised here,
The other held up.

Then the wave breaks and they are dashed
To their sure end,
To foam, to sea, to nothingness,
To drift, to mend.

They paddle in the shallows still;
Two splash each other;
Then all swim out to wait until
The right waves gather.

What is the poet’s main purpose in this poem?
 
  1. To explain the science of how waves form
  2. To celebrate the beauty and bravery of surfers riding the waves
  3. To warn readers about the dangers of the ocean
  4. To tell a humorous story about a day at the beach

by Bruce Dawe

One day soon he’ll tell her it’s time to start packing,
and the kids will yell “Truly?” and get wildly excited for no reason,
and the brown kelpie pup will start dashing about, tripping everyone up,
and she’ll go out to the vegetable patch and pick all the green tomatoes from the bushes
to take with her; and notice how the oldest girl is close to tears
because she was happy here, and how the youngest girl is beaming
because she wasn’t.

And the first thing she’ll put on the trailer will be the bottling set
she never unpacked from Grovedale, and when the loaded ute bumps down the drive
past the blackberry canes with their last shrivelled fruit,
she won’t even ask why they’re leaving this time, or where they’re heading for –
she’ll only remember how, when they came here, she held out her hands
bright with berries, the first of the season,
and said: Make a wish, Tom, make a wish.

What is the poet’s main purpose in this poem?
 
  1. To explain why farming families move so often
  2. To describe the excitement of moving house
  3. To capture the mixed emotions of a family who must move again
  4. To persuade people to travel around Australia

Slow Reader

by Vicki Feaver

He can make sculptures
and fabulous machines,
invent games, tell jokes,
give solemn, adult advice—
but he is slow to read.

When I take him on my knee
with his Ladybird book
he gazes into the air,
sighing and shaking his head
like an old man who knows
the mountains are impassable.

He toys with words,
letting them go cold
as gristly meat,
until I relent and let him wriggle free:
a fish returning to its element,
or a white-eyed colt—
shying from the bit—
who sees that if he takes it in his mouth
he’ll never run quite free again.

What is the poet’s main purpose in this poem?
 
  1. To criticise children who are unwilling to read
  2. To show that creative talents may not match reading ability
  3. To explain why some children dislike school
  4. To persuade parents to read more to their children

Slow Reader

by Vicki Feaver

He can make sculptures
and fabulous machines,
invent games, tell jokes,
give solemn, adult advice—
but he is slow to read.

When I take him on my knee
with his Ladybird book
he gazes into the air,
sighing and shaking his head
like an old man who knows
the mountains are impassable.

He toys with words,
letting them go cold
as gristly meat,
until I relent and let him wriggle free:
a fish returning to its element,
or a white-eyed colt—
shying from the bit—
who sees that if he takes it in his mouth
he’ll never run quite free again.

What does the image of a “white-eyed colt—shying from the bit” symbolise?
 
  1. A child who is wild and resists being controlled by reading
  2. A boy who enjoys horses more than books
  3. The idea that reading is like a race to be won
  4. The boy’s fear of animals and farm life

Snake
by David Campbell

The tiger snake moves
Like slow lightning. Like
A yard of creek water
It flows over rocks
Carving the grass.

Where have you gone,
Long fellow, cold brother,
Like a lopped limb or
Truth that we shy from
Leaving a cast skin?

Snakes are like a line
Of poetry: a chill
Wind in the noon,
A slalom in the spine
Setting ears back, hair on end.

‘Some people will not live
With a snake in the house.’
Mice make off. Look
Under your chair; worse —
Take down a book:
A line like an icicle!
How does the description of the snake as “cold brother” contrast with its image as “a line of poetry”?
 
  1. One suggests family closeness, the other suggests artistic distance
  2. One emphasises fear, the other suggests beauty and inspiration
  3. Both highlight warmth, but in different ways
  4. Both describe the snake as ordinary and unnoticed
