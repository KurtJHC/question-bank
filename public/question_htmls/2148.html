washed down after summer thunder,
at the shop
with dead flies in the window display,
at the mosquito mangroves
and the sucking sound of mud crabs,
at the children throwing mulberries
the stain like lipstick.
The big river
rolls past our town,
takes a slow look,
and rolls away.

The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn follows the life of a young adventurous boy (Huck Finn) as he runs away from both his guardian, the Widow Douglas and his father Pap. He accidentally teams up with another runaway, a slave named Jim. As the two set off they hope to find freedom from different things; Huck from both the thought of being “civilised” and his drunken father, and Jim from his current life. Jim is a slave and is running away to become a free man, so he can in turn, free his family from slavery. 

The two unlikely friends journey down the Mississippi river in what turns out to be a series of adventures filled with death, disguise and eventually the growth and maturity of their friendship and of Huck himself. The river is an important symbol and is the agent that brings the two closer to their destination. 

The Mississippi is the fourth largest river in the world and stretches over ten US states. Twain retells his experiences on the Mississippi river in a memoir published in 1883, ‘Life on the Mississippi’, centered on his life as a steamboat pilot. It follows his journeys on the Mississippi from St Louis to New Orleans. His own experiences were what inspired the 1834 novel. Twain’s recount of Huck’s journey along the river maps the road to the young boy discovering himself. Twain’s love for the Mississippi river is no doubt evident in the novel. It is portrayed as not just a long and winding trail that leads out of town and away from potential conflicts, but also a path that enables one to find oneself.

There’s a reason why the sound of a river or a creek calms your soul. Even the sight of it calms you. It is because the river has come to teach us all one of the most powerful lessons on earth: No one’s words or thoughts can derail us from our goals.

The river is a great teacher. Round up every high school bully, every negative person who wants the worst for others and have them congregate around the river. Then, have them look downward into its clear moving water. As they look down they can see their faces looking back up at them. They begin chanting at the river with every fibre of their mind and spirit: “You’re not good enough.” “You’re wasting your time, you’ll never make it.” 

What other negative things might they be saying? Yet, even while all of this profound negativity is being channelled toward the river, it continues to flow. The river moves no matter what the atmosphere. If it’s raining, lightning or grey – it moves. The river channels all of its mind, body and spirit into the direction of its dreams. When the river goes through a break up with its beloved – it doesn’t stop moving. It flows. It’s relentless. It’s wise. 

One thing you can be certain of about the river is that it never dwells on the past. Ever. Do you hear the sound of the water rushing over the rocks? Tell me… when you hear the babbling water, when are you hearing it; in the past or the future? Neither. It’s only possible to hear this precious sound in the now. The river understands by letting go of the past entirely, allows for its potential in the present to reach its absolute zenith. We all can take away great wisdom from the river. It personifies the truth that moving while challenged is at the core of what wisdom really is.

Rivers, as the source of life are reflected in the myths and beliefs of the multitude of cultures. In many parts of the world rivers are called “mothers”. Narmadai River in India, “Mother Narmada”; Volga River in Russia is Mat Rodnaya, “Mother Earth”. The Thai word ‘mae nan’ is translated as “water mother”. 

There is something poetic in the life of rivers. It is too complex to describe the spiritual connection between a person and a river. The melting snow and pouring rain on tops of mountains and hills, form beautiful mountain streams that flow in the high grounds. As the streams power down the tributaries, they gain their volume and become a river. As they leave the high grounds, rivers slow down and begin to meander, seeking the path of least resistance through wide valleys. Finally, the river flows into a lake or ocean. 

All the big milestones in the human history have taken place around riverbanks. The first civilisation emerged +/- 3000 BC along the Euphrates, Tigris, Nile, and Indus; and later along the Yellow River. The industrial revolution occurred along rivers in northern England which contributed to the development of industry. Most large cities in the world are located on a river. An interesting fact is that 65% of the water we drink comes from a surrounding river. It is another great reason to keep them clean.
