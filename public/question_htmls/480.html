Explorer
by Alan Brownjohn


Two o’clock:
Let out of the back door of the house, our cat
Is practicing the snow.


The layer of white makes a small, straight, crumbling cliff
Where we open the back door inwards. The cat
Sniffs it with suspicion, learns you can just about
Pat the flaking snow with a careful dab. Then,
A little bolder, he dints it with one whole foot
—and withdraws it, curls it as if slightly lame,


And looks down at it, oddly. The snow is
Different from anything else, not like
A rug, or a stretch of lino,1 or an armchair to claw upon
And be told to Get off!


The snow is peculiar, but not forbidden. The cat
Is welcome to go out in the snow. Does
The snow welcome the cat?
He thinks, looks, tries again.


Three paces out of the door, his white feet find
You sink a little way all the time, it is slow and cold, but it
Doesn’t particularly hurt. Perhaps you can even enjoy it, as something new.
So he walks on, precisely, on the tips of very cautious paws . . .


Half-past three, the cat stretched warm indoors.
From the bedroom window we can see his explorations
—From door to fence, from fence to gate, from gate to wall to tree, and back,
Are long pattered tracks and trade-routes of round paw-marks
Which fresh snow is quietly filling.

A String of Sausages
by Katrina Bufe

 

     “Ruth, where are you?” Mother called from the kitchen.

     Ruth finished putting away her clothes. “I’ll be right there,” she yelled.

     Mother had them all housecleaning today, while Father rowed the twelve miles to the harbor for their winter supplies. There was no road to the little village from the Big Sable Lighthouse on Lake Superior; the only way to get provisions was by boat. Harsh storms made travel over the lake impossible in winter, so Father was making the last trip to town until spring. Their dog Brit had gone with him.

     When Ruth came into the kitchen, Mother said, “Go tell Win it’s time to read the weather and write the journal. He’s in the backyard.”

     “Oh, Mother, please let me do it,” Ruth pleaded. “I know I can do it right and I write neater than Win does.”

     “But it’s important that the instruments be read properly, Ruth. You’d better have Win do it.” Seeing Ruth’s disappointed face, she added, “You can help him.”

     Ruth loved to check the weather. Father had long ago taught her to read a thermometer and a barometer and to figure the direction and speed of the wind. Sometimes she was still off by a degree or two or unsure about the wind speed, so Ruth wasn’t allowed to write the journal herself. Thirteen-year-old Win’s readings were always correct.

     Ruth found her brothers chopping wood and gave Win the message. “And Mother said I’m supposed to help,” she finished.

     Both boys were glad to stop. Ban went into the house, and Ruth and Win headed up the spiral stairs of the lighthouse tower. On the second landing Ruth pulled out a wooden writing board built into the curved wall beneath the window and took out the ink, pen, and journal from the cupboard next to it. Placing the book on the writing board, she opened to the last entry. She and Father had made it just that morning.

     November 4, 1891

     7 A.M. winds E-NE, 3 knots, lake
     calm, barometer reads 30.05 and steady,
     temperature 45°F, clear skies. Taking the
     day to row to Grand Marais for supplies.

     Win carefully opened the bottle of ink, dipped the pen, and began to read the thermometer. “The temperature’s dropped. I thought it felt cooler,” he said.

     Ruth tried reading the thermometer and barometer while Win calculated the wind speed. The temperature was 40°F. Ruth read the barometer and gasped. “Win! The barometer
has fallen to 28!”

     “What? Are you sure? Let me see.” He studied the instrument and figured out loud. “Twenty . . . eight . . . point zero, zero.” He looked at Ruth. “Father says it never gets much lower than 29, ever. We are in for some storm.” Ruth and Win looked at each other and thought the same thing. Father.

     The four of them ate their noon meal late, in silence. The wind was coming harder from the south. Father would have started rowing back from town now, Ruth thought. Maybe he could make it back before the storm hit. Or maybe he’d decided to stay in the village. Ruth hoped so.

     All afternoon Mother kept them busy. The boys stacked wood, and Ruth washed dishes, made beds, and scrubbed floors. Ban started a fire in the living room fireplace as the house grew chillier. Finally, when there was nothing left to do, they sat by the fire watching the flames.

     Suddenly a windblast from the northwest struck the house, rattling the shutters. The windows went dark and snow pelted the panes. The blizzard had surrounded them.

     “Go light the big lantern, Win,” Mother said. “It’ll be needed in this storm.”

     Win took the kerosene jug and the matches and hurried out the door that connected the house and tower. When he came back, Mother said cheerfully, “Let’s surprise Father with a special meal. He’ll be tired and hungry from the hard rowing. We can use whatever we have left in the pantry. What would you like?”

     Ban wanted fish, Win chose fried potatoes, and Ruth picked corn bread.

     “Good. I’ll make a blueberry pie. I think there’s just enough flour and lard left,” Mother said. “Ruth, go in the pantry and check. You boys can peel potatoes.”

     It was nearly six o’clock when they had finished. The pie was in the oven. Preparing the meal had kept them from thinking too much, but now Ruth began to worry more. They knew Father was late. What if he didn’t get there at all

     Mother smiled reassuringly. “Win, check the light once more before dinner. Father won’t want to do that when he gets back.” As she finished speaking, the bang of the door and the thump of feet came from the entryway, followed by a muffled voice.

     “Well, Brit, you’d think someone would come to welcome us back.”

     Ruth ran to the door and opened it. There stood a white statue of snow and ice. A smaller white figure wagged its tail, let out a whimper, and dropped, exhausted, to the floor.

     “Otto!” Mother exclaimed as she quickly came to the door. “You made it!”

     “Barely,” Father managed to speak. “Help me out of these frozen clothes. I’ve never seen such a severe storm hit so hard, so fast.”

     Ban carried Brit to the fire while Ruth ran to get blankets and Win went to check the light. After Father had put on dry clothes and was wrapped in blankets by the fire, he began to explain.

     “I left Grand Marais as soon as I saw the wind switch to the south. But I left too late for this storm. I was loaded to the gills with our winter supplies. I had gotten everything on
our list and then some. I was just two miles from home when the wind switched to the northwest. I should have rammed the boat on the shore and walked the rest of the way. I would have gotten here hours ago and in better shape. But I wanted to save the boat. What would I do if a ship wrecked, with no boat? I didn’t want to lose our winter supplies either. I tried to decide what to do as I headed the boat into one large wave after another.”

     Father paused to sip his coffee. “Finally the lake decided for me. As I pointed the prow into a wave as high as a wall, a second wave came on the right side of me, and one just as big came at me from behind. And three waves crashed down together, and I was thrown from the boat. It washed up on shore just a little ways down from where I ended up. I managed to pull it to a safe place and I think it can be fixed. But everything that was in it has washed away.”

     “Everything?” Ruth asked.

     “Yes. I never even saw anything floating on the water or washed up on shore.” Father turned to Mother. “How are we doing for food?”

     Ruth’s stomach felt queasy. She wasn’t hungry anymore.

     “We’ll manage,” Mother said. Ruth glanced at her—they both knew the fl our and sugar bins were nearly empty—but Mother’s look told her to be quiet. “Just so we’re all together. We’ll do fine.”

     “I was sure we’d lost Brit,” Father continued. “I looked and called for her until I knew I had to start home or I’d freeze to death. Just as I turned to leave, I saw a speck moving down the beach. I started walking toward it, and there was Brit running to me!” They looked at the dog sleeping peacefully on Ban’s lap.

     “And I was wrong to tell you everything was lost. Bring me my coat, Ruth,” Father said. When she brought the soggy jacket, he started digging through the pockets. “Here . . . this is what Brit was carrying in her mouth.”

     “A string of sausages!” Ruth exclaimed. “Frozen solid.”

     Father chuckled. “I’m not sure, but I think she intends to share them.”

     A string of sausages wasn’t much to live on for the winter. But Father said they would all search the shore tomorrow for any supplies that might have washed up during the night.

     After Father finished his story, Mother told him about the special dinner. They remembered the pie in the oven just in time. Nothing was spared; Mother wouldn’t allow it. “Tonight we celebrate Father’s coming home safely.”

     Mother walked over to Brit and patted her head. “And I’m cooking a sausage just for you!”

     The O’Brien home was one of the few examples of old-style wooden structures that hadn’t been demolished in Boston’s urban-renewal campaign at the turn of the century. Old man O’Brien had no heirs, so when he died the family home went up for auction, and the Urban Center bought it. When local officials arrived for an appraisal, they discovered that the house had a backyard, which is forbidden by zoning restrictions.

When the news of the tree’s discovery leaked out, quite a few sightseers stopped by to have a look at it, and the local government, realizing the money-making potential, began charging admission and advertising the place. By now it had become a favorite spot for school field trips and family excursions such as ours.

     When we arrived in main Boston we rode the elevator-bus up to ground level and caught a monorail out to East Boston Urban Center 3. An aircush taxi took us the rest of the way to the residence.

     The home itself was unimpressive. It had none of the marble gloss or steely sheen of modern buildings, but was rather a dull white color, with the paint peeling in places. Dad paid the admission fee, and we spent the next f if teen minutes on a dull guided tour of the house. The rooms were roped off to keep people from touching anything, but there were no windows facing the illegal backyard anyway, so it really didn’t matter that I couldn’t enter the rooms on that side.

     My mind was on the tree, and I thought the inside tour would never end, but soon we were walking through a doorway hidden in one of the bookshelves and into the backyard. The yard was big—at least ten by twenty feet—and I was surprised to find real grass growing on the sides of the concrete walkway built for tourists. The grass didn’t distract me for long, however, because I just couldn’t help noticing the tree!

     It was located at one end of the yard, and there was a mesh fence around it for protection. It was similar in form to the plastic trees I’d seen, but there was much more to it than that. But best of all was the smell. It was a fresh, living odor, alien to the antiseptic world outside with all its metal, plastic, and glass. The three of us stood there for a moment, and then the tour guide told us to make room for the next group. I didn’t want to go—in fact, I almost felt like crying.

     On the way back, Mom and Dad were silent, and I read through one of the brochures the guide had passed out. When I came to the part that said the O’Brien home would be open only for the rest of this year, I was sad. They intend to tear down the place to make room for some kind of insurance building, and the tree will have to go, too.

     For the rest of the trip I just sat still, fingering the object in my pocket which I had picked off the grass in the O’Briens’ backyard. I think it’s called an acorn.

The First Woman Doctor

 

     Unlike many people in the 1800s, Elizabeth Blackwell’s parents believed in an equal education for their daughters and sons. All the Blackwell children were educated by tutors at home and were encouraged to be independent. This gave Elizabeth Blackwell the courage to become a doctor, something that had never been accomplished by a woman in the United States. Intelligent and strong-willed, she was determined to achieve this milestone.

A Different Idea

     When Blackwell was 24 years old, she took care of a family friend who had fallen ill. The friend suggested that Blackwell become a doctor. Blackwell had never thought about studying medicine and was initially reluctant to consider the idea. After thinking it over, however, Blackwell decided that becoming a doctor was worthwhile. She accepted the challenge with alacrity, excited at the prospect of going to medical school.

     Blackwell applied to medical schools in Philadelphia and New York. Most schools did not think she was serious, nor did they have confidence in her ability. Consequently, they gave no credence to her applications. After 28 schools rejected her, the Geneva Medical School of Western New York accepted her as a student only because it was assumed that a rival medical school had submitted her application as a joke. Blackwell ultimately graduated at the top of her class, becoming the first female doctor in the United States.

A New Kind of Medical Care

     Later, while studying in a hospital in England, Blackwell met Florence Nightingale. Nightingale was a nursing pioneer, and Blackwell was impressed with her practice of controlling illness through cleanliness. Blackwell returned to New York to set up her medical practice and began writing and lecturing on the need for good nutrition and sanitation to fight disease. Because of these efforts, Blackwell is thought to have been far ahead of her time. Doctors did not know then, as they do today, that many illnesses are caused by unsanitary conditions.

     Blackwell also wanted to treat patients who could not afford health care. In 1857 she opened the New York Infirmary for Women and Children, offering medical care to indigent patients. Focusing on the need for sanitation, she established a position at the hospital called a sanitary visitor. This position was the forerunner of the modern social worker. Sanitary visitors went to people’s homes to discuss cleanliness and disease prevention. The hospital was a great success and treated many patients, but Blackwell still had other goals to accomplish.

More Achievements

     In 1858 Blackwell returned to England for further study. A year later she became the first woman to be named to the British Medical Register, a listing of British doctors. Later she returned to New York, where she established the Women’s Medical College of the New York Infirmary in 1868. Like many of Blackwell’s undertakings, the college was ahead of its time. It required students to take an entrance exam, practice working with patients, and submit to a three-year course of study. Other medical schools at that time required as little as ten months of training.

An Inspiration to Others

     Blackwell’s achievements attracted other accomplished women to work with her. One of the first African American doctors was Rebecca Cole, who started out as a sanitary visitor for Blackwell. Blackwell’s younger sister Emily was inspired to become a doctor as well and worked with Blackwell at the New York Infirmary.

     Through her dedication and determination, Elizabeth Blackwell achieved many of her goals. In 1869 she moved to England and established a practice in London from which she eventually retired. Today at Hobart and William Smith College in Geneva, New York, formerly the Geneva Medical School from which she graduated, a beautiful bronze statue honors Elizabeth Blackwell, a medical pioneer who influenced others to pursue their dreams.

     When Blackwell was 24 years old, she took care of a family friend who had fallen ill. The friend suggested that Blackwell become a doctor. Blackwell had never thought about studying medicine and was initially reluctant to consider the idea. After thinking it over, however, Blackwell decided that becoming a doctor was worthwhile. She accepted the challenge with alacrity, excited at the prospect of going to medical school.

     Blackwell applied to medical schools in Philadelphia and New York. Most schools did not think she was serious, nor did they have confidence in her ability. Consequently, they gave no credence to her applications. After 28 schools rejected her, the Geneva Medical School of Western New York accepted her as a student only because it was assumed that a rival medical school had submitted her application as a joke. Blackwell ultimately graduated at the top of her class, becoming the first female doctor in the United States.

A New Kind of Medical Care

     Later, while studying in a hospital in England, Blackwell met Florence Nightingale. Nightingale was a nursing pioneer, and Blackwell was impressed with her practice of controlling illness through cleanliness. Blackwell returned to New York to set up her medical practice and began writing and lecturing on the need for good nutrition and sanitation to fight disease. Because of these efforts, Blackwell is thought to have been far ahead of her time. Doctors did not know then, as they do today, that many illnesses are caused by unsanitary conditions.

     Blackwell also wanted to treat patients who could not afford health care. In 1857 she opened the New York Infirmary for Women and Children, offering medical care to indigent patients. Focusing on the need for sanitation, she established a position at the hospital called a sanitary visitor. This position was the forerunner of the modern social worker. Sanitary visitors went to people’s homes to discuss cleanliness and disease prevention. The hospital was a great success and treated many patients, but Blackwell still had other goals to accomplish.

More Achievements

     In 1858 Blackwell returned to England for further study. A year later she became the first woman to be named to the British Medical Register, a listing of British doctors. Later she returned to New York, where she established the Women’s Medical College of the New York Infirmary in 1868. Like many of Blackwell’s undertakings, the college was ahead of its time. It required students to take an entrance exam, practice working with patients, and submit to a three-year course of study. Other medical schools at that time required as little as ten months of training.

An Inspiration to Others

     Blackwell’s achievements attracted other accomplished women to work with her. One of the first African American doctors was Rebecca Cole, who started out as a sanitary visitor for Blackwell. Blackwell’s younger sister Emily was inspired to become a doctor as well and worked with Blackwell at the New York Infirmary.

     Through her dedication and determination, Elizabeth Blackwell achieved many of her goals. In 1869 she moved to England and established a practice in London from which she eventually retired. Today at Hobart and William Smith College in Geneva, New York, formerly the Geneva Medical School from which she graduated, a beautiful bronze statue honors Elizabeth Blackwell, a medical pioneer who influenced others to pursue their dreams.
