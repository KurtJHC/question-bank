Painted Into a Corner

by Amy Bearce

 

1     I didn’t know which was more humiliating— watching all of the potential customers walk right past my table or seeing them line up at Julie’s table. I sat back and pretended I couldn’t care less, but the truth was I was hurt.

2     Two weeks ago my teacher began class by announcing a new project. We had to create a product to sell to the community at the Business Day Fair in the gym. All the students in the class had to design and create something to sell. We also had to keep track of the costs of the product as well as the profit we made. Whoever made the most profit would win the Young Business Leader award.

3     Immediately my mind began soaring. Perhaps I could make beautiful jewelry that everyone would pay a fortune for. Or maybe I could sew pajamas and sell those. Of course, I’d have to learn how to sew first. I imagined myself with hundreds of customers lined up at my table, my Young Business Leader trophy sitting next to me, shining in the beautiful sunlight. . . .

4     That night I shared my ideas with my mom. Smiling, she broke the news to me that using silver wire and beads as the raw material for my proposed jewelry project would be expensive. Moreover, the idea for pajamas was flawed because I already had a reputation as someone who couldn’t even sew a button on a shirt.

5     With cold water thrown on my first two ideas, I finally came up with a plan we both thought would work. I would create and sell decorative ceramic ornaments. I had been in a ceramics class all summer, so I knew that I could get plain ceramic ornaments and paint them. All I would have left to do would be to install a hook and—presto!

6     The next morning at school I ran up to Julie, my best friend, and said, “Hey, have you come up with an idea for Business Day?”

7     “Yes,” she said, “but I’m not telling anyone what it is before the sale.”

8     I blinked and felt a pang in my stomach. Julie and I usually shared everything. Why was she being so cautious?

9     Stiffly I replied, “Oh, well, I have an idea too,” and quickly walked away. I couldn’t believe that my best friend was keeping secrets from me.

  1.  The narrator
  2.  The narrator’s mum
  3.  The narrator’s best friend
  4.  The narrator’s teacher

Marco Polo and the “Million Lies”

by Nancy Beaulieu

 

Young Adventurer

Marco Polo was born in Venice, Italy, in 1254. He was 17 when he set out for China with his father and uncle, a journey which lasted more than three years. The three travelers passed through vast deserts and across rugged mountain ranges. Marco met people from different lands and saw wondrous new places. He saw camels, which he called oxen with humps, and mountain goats, which he described as sheep with long curled horns. He heard the music of the sand dunes shifting in the wind. He climbed mountains so high that the thin air made him dizzy. Marco was fascinated by everything he saw.

Marco Polo and his family reached China in 1275. At that time, Kublai Khan was the emperor. He was impressed with Marco and put the young man to work. Marco spent more than 16 years in Khan’s service. He went on missions to faraway lands and entertained Khan with stories of his travels. Then, in 1292, Marco asked for permission to return to Venice. Khan did not want to see Marco go, but he finally agreed. Marco and his family returned to Venice.

 

“Marco Milione”

The Travels of Marco Polo was a popular book, but it did not earn Marco much respect. It was often referred to as “Il Milione.” By this the Italians meant “the million lies.” Some people even called him “Marco Milione.” Many of Marco’s stories were hard to believe. Besides the unusual animals he described, Marco told of other things difficult for most Europeans to imagine. For example, Marco described how the Chinese people read books printed on paper and used paper money. At that time, paper was rare in Europe. To people reading about Marco’s adventures, paper money and books printed on paper sounded like something from a fable.

Marco also described a system that Khan’s empire used to rapidly deliver messages to faraway places. Marco’s countrymen thought this sounded impossible too. Who could blame them? It would be hundreds of years before a mail system was used in Europe. Even details about daily life in China seemed made up. Marco told about delicate china serving bowls that ordinary people used. At a time when most Europeans did not have silverware, this seemed unbelievable. Everyday clothes made from silk sounded like a fairy tale to Europeans, since most of them had just one change of woolen clothes.

 

Misread and Misunderstood

Much of what Marco described in his stories was later proven true. However, there are some parts where Marco’s memory may have been foggy, or Rustichello may have embellished the truth. Certainly no one else has ever seen any large birds that drop elephants from great heights and then eat the remains! It is also strange that Marco did not describe something as amazing as the Great Wall of China, which he must have seen.

Whatever the complete truth about his book, one thing is certain: Marco Polo was a great explorer and adventurer.

  3.  “Then, in 1292, Marco asked for permission to return to Venice.”
  4.  “Khan did not want to see Marco go, but he finally agreed.”
