What Time Is This Place?

by the Research and Educational Planning Center

Introduction

1   Photographs, maps, oral accounts, and written records are not the only clues to the past. Buildings and places are records, too. Old buildings are part of a community’s past as well as its present. Long after its original occupants are gone, a building remains as a reminder of the human activities it once enclosed. Remodeling may make an old building look modern or adjust it for new uses, but something of the past is always there. A building’s location, the materials used to construct it, the way it looks, and its uses all reflect life in a town as it used to be.

Locations Of Buildings

Most towns expanded around a main street. This part of town was busy with foot traffic, vehicles, children, pigs, and dogs. Hotels, blacksmith shops, saloons, livery stables, restaurants, and government buildings lined main street. It was here that railroads and stagelines had their offices. Property was expensive on main street, so land was not wasted. Buildings were huddled together and expanded up, rather than out.

Private homes and boarding houses surrounded the business district. Working people who walked to work lived in residences near their jobs. Those who could afford to own carriages and horses lived at the edge of town away from the noise and dirt of main street. Large lots and yards separated one home from another. Churches and schools were usually built in residential areas.

With the introduction of street cars and automobiles, people could move farther from the center of town. They no longer had to live near work or shopping areas. Streets were made longer and wider for automobiles, and parking lots and garages replaced the livery stables and blacksmith shops. Buildings could be put almost anywhere. Distance was no longer a big problem.

Building Materials

5   In Nevada, building materials were scarce. There were few forests to provide lumber and no quick and easy supply of bricks and cement. Some of the earliest buildings were made of sun dried mud and straw bricks called adobe. Very few of these adobe buildings are left any more. Wind and water have washed their walls back into the soil.

As people streamed into the state seeking quick mining riches, they would build “rag towns” with materials at hand. Shelters were made from cloth which was stretched tent-like over wooden frames. When available, logs and stones were used as building materials. Even large pieces of metal were put up to protect against the weather.

When mining booms began, more services as well as more buildings were needed. Bricklayers, carpenters, and other craftsmen came to build. Stone quarries were opened. Brick ovens were built. Wood was cut from the Sierra forests and shipped throughout the state by wagon or railroad. Houses, barns, businesses, churches, and govern ment buildings were built for the quickly expanding population.

Style

Buildings are much like people. They come in different shapes, sizes, and colors. They do different things, and they have different moods. Some are bright and delicate with frills everywhere. They seem playful and inviting. Others are dark, sturdy blocks that take themselves very seriously. Once in a while, you might meet a quiet, plain building that looks like an old friend. To a h istorian, all of these things are part of a building’s style.

Style is a clue to a building’s age. It also tells something about what was thought to be “good taste” by those who built and used the structure. There are special words to describe a building’s style and parts of a building.

10   Some names come from the period when the building and others like it were built. Some come from earlier periods; these were copied or revived (renewed). Most buildings in Nevada are a mixture of styles. However, two styles strongly influenced early Nevada builders, Greek Revival and Victorian.

From 1820 to 1840, it was very popular in the United States to have buildings look like Greek temples. The special marks of this style, called Greek Revival, are pillared porches, low, sometimes flat roofs, corniced eaves, and pediments. Many public buildings and mansions in Washington, D.C. were Greek Revival, and this style was brought west with settlers. It was in fashion in California and Nevada by 1860.

A Greek Revival building is simple and square. A Victorian one is an explosion of rooms, angles, and wooden icicles. The style was named for the period from 1836 to about 1900 when Queen Victor ia’s taste ruled England and America. Victorian buildings have steeply pitched roofs with barge-board trim and arched windows. Structures move up and out with bays and additions. Carpenters could buy books with floor plans and ready made trim patterns. These patterns could be cut cheaply out of wood to decorate a house.

What Time Is This Place?

by the Research and Educational Planning Center

Introduction

1   Photographs, maps, oral accounts, and written records are not the only clues to the past. Buildings and places are records, too. Old buildings are part of a community’s past as well as its present. Long after its original occupants are gone, a building remains as a reminder of the human activities it once enclosed. Remodeling may make an old building look modern or adjust it for new uses, but something of the past is always there. A building’s location, the materials used to construct it, the way it looks, and its uses all reflect life in a town as it used to be.

Locations Of Buildings

Most towns expanded around a main street. This part of town was busy with foot traffic, vehicles, children, pigs, and dogs. Hotels, blacksmith shops, saloons, livery stables, restaurants, and government buildings lined main street. It was here that railroads and stagelines had their offices. Property was expensive on main street, so land was not wasted. Buildings were huddled together and expanded up, rather than out.

Private homes and boarding houses surrounded the business district. Working people who walked to work lived in residences near their jobs. Those who could afford to own carriages and horses lived at the edge of town away from the noise and dirt of main street. Large lots and yards separated one home from another. Churches and schools were usually built in residential areas.

With the introduction of street cars and automobiles, people could move farther from the center of town. They no longer had to live near work or shopping areas. Streets were made longer and wider for automobiles, and parking lots and garages replaced the livery stables and blacksmith shops. Buildings could be put almost anywhere. Distance was no longer a big problem.

Building Materials

5   In Nevada, building materials were scarce. There were few forests to provide lumber and no quick and easy supply of bricks and cement. Some of the earliest buildings were made of sun dried mud and straw bricks called adobe. Very few of these adobe buildings are left any more. Wind and water have washed their walls back into the soil.

As people streamed into the state seeking quick mining riches, they would build “rag towns” with materials at hand. Shelters were made from cloth which was stretched tent-like over wooden frames. When available, logs and stones were used as building materials. Even large pieces of metal were put up to protect against the weather.

When mining booms began, more services as well as more buildings were needed. Bricklayers, carpenters, and other craftsmen came to build. Stone quarries were opened. Brick ovens were built. Wood was cut from the Sierra forests and shipped throughout the state by wagon or railroad. Houses, barns, businesses, churches, and govern ment buildings were built for the quickly expanding population.

Style

Buildings are much like people. They come in different shapes, sizes, and colors. They do different things, and they have different moods. Some are bright and delicate with frills everywhere. They seem playful and inviting. Others are dark, sturdy blocks that take themselves very seriously. Once in a while, you might meet a quiet, plain building that looks like an old friend.
