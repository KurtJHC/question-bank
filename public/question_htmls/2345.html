NOT IN INDIA

‘I want a proper bag,’ I cried.
‘But this is a proper bag.
In India we always took these to school.’
‘We’re not in India!’

‘Go on, go to school, Craig is here,’ ordered Mum.
Craig, his clean pink face beaming a smile,
had his bag across his back.
My satchel laboured at my hip,
constantly trying to get away.

We arrive in school, late for prayers again. Good.
Spelling test. Easy. Full marks.
‘This boy came to Britain when he was six.
He couldn’t speak English.
Now he’s a better speller than all of you.’

Maths, just long division and fractions.
‘Indians are always good at Maths,’ Mum used to say.
Geography. Maps. Places I haven’t seen.
‘Sadi came to Wales two years ago and now he knows
all the countries in the world.’

Leaving school for another day.
A boy runs shouting ‘Chocolate!’
Craig defends me, ‘Then you’re a Milky Bar!’
‘But I’m not chocolate, I’m just like you!’ I think.

‘Home already?’
In India we didn’t come home ‘til five,’ said Mum,
‘But we’re not in India, are we?’ I cried,
running upstairs and locking the door behind me.

  3. The complex relationship immigrant children can have with trying to fit into their new culture and surroundings, whilst simultaneously trying to distance themselves from their past identities. 
  4. All of the above

Traditional Aboriginal Lifestyle - Darug

The Darug people were the original inhabitants of the Sydney Basin, living sustainably for millennia through a lifestyle deeply connected to the land and its resources. Their territory encompassed a significant area, including the regions now known as Sydney, the Blue Mountains, and the Hawkesbury River. Living in small family groups, their movements were dictated by the seasons and the availability of food. (A)................. This profound understanding of their environment ensured they utilised resources effectively without causing long-term harm. Their rich cultural life was intrinsically linked to the Dreaming, a system of beliefs and stories that guided their interactions with the land and each other. Elders played a vital role in transmitting this cultural knowledge through oral traditions, while ceremonies marked significant life stages and seasonal changes. The Darug language, though significantly impacted by European settlement, is experiencing a revival, with remnants visible in local place names. (B).................

The arrival of the British in 1788 had a devastating impact, leading to land dispossession, disruption of traditional food sources, the introduction of diseases, and forced displacement. Despite these immense challenges, the descendants of the Darug people have shown remarkable resilience and continue to work towards cultural preservation. Their traditional methods of sustaining life demonstrated an intricate knowledge of the environment. They followed carefully planned movements to access fresh food and water, possessing a detailed understanding of bush tucker, including edible and medicinal plants. (C)................. This allowed them to thrive in their environment without causing lasting ecological damage.

The Darug people were skilled in crafting a range of tools and weapons essential for their daily lives. Spears and boomerangs were used for hunting, while digging sticks and woven baskets aided in gathering plant-based foods. Stone tools were crucial for various tasks, including cutting, shaping materials, and preparing food. (D)................. 

Their society was structured around small family groups or clans, connected by intricate kinship systems that fostered cooperation and stability. Elders held positions of great respect, responsible for decision-making and the transmission of cultural knowledge, while men and women fulfilled distinct yet equally important roles within the community.

Art, music, and storytelling were fundamental to Darug culture. Rock carvings and paintings served as visual records of the Dreaming and important events. Music and dance were integral to ceremonies and gatherings, often carrying deep spiritual meaning. (E)................. These traditions played a crucial role in reinforcing their values, laws, and understanding of their heritage. Spirituality permeated their way of life, with the Dreaming providing a comprehensive framework for their beliefs and practices. Ceremonies, such as initiations, honoured ancestors and marked significant transitions. Sacred sites across their land held profound spiritual importance.

Today, the Darug people are actively involved in revitalising their culture, with initiatives focused on language revival, the protection of sacred sites, and the education of both Indigenous and non-Indigenous Australians about their history and traditions. How do we write about fear in a story? Fear is a complicated emotion that involves strong emotional, physiological, and behavioural elements. To write fear well means you’re going to need to address each of those elements, while also taking into consideration the character’s personal history and why their fears developed. Like any of the major emotions, fear has a way of creeping into stories of just about every genre. If you’re writing a romance, you may have a character feel afraid of rejection as they plan to profess their love. If you’re writing something with action or horror elements, a character may encounter another person who poses a threat to their safety. In any story, however, a character may be afraid of bees or heights, or other things they could encounter at any time. If you can’t properly convey the feeling of fear in these situations, an otherwise good scene could end up feeling very shallow. There are two major types of fear: rational and irrational. Rational fears are fears that make sense, such as being afraid of death or something that could cause serious bodily harm. In most cases, rational fears arise from genuine dangers; if there’s a good reason to be afraid of something, then it’s a rational fear to have—otherwise known as phobias.  Irrational fears are fears that don’t make sense, and they vary from person to person. Someone might be afraid of clowns, even though clowns pose no real danger to anyone’s personal safety.
