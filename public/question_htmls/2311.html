Australian Crocodiles

  • Crocodiles are found in the hot, tropical northern part of the continent.
  • Both crocodiles grab their prey and move to deep water, where they roll over to drown the animal.
  • Crocodiles have long narrow snouts and the 4th tooth of the lower jaw is outside when the mouth is closed.
  • Crocodiles have very strong jaws.
  • In the stomach the food is broken down.
  • On land, crocodiles can run very quickly, lifting their bodies up off the ground.
  • One kind lives in the sea and the other kind lives in fresh water.
  • The Estuarine crocodile grows to between 4 and 7 metres long.
  • The Estuarine crocodile, while it lives in the sea, is able to survive quite far upriver in fresh water.
  • The Estuarine is one of the most dangerous of all the crocodile family, being the biggest and heaviest.
  • The eyes and nostrils are on the top part of the head so that they can lie in the water almost completely hidden from view.
  • The Johnson’s crocodile grows up to 3 metres long. It is considered to be dangerous even though it is not known for attacking humans.
  • The legs are short with webbed toes, five on the front legs and four on the back legs.
  • There are two kinds of crocodiles in Australia: The Estuarine, a saltwater crocodile and Johnson’s, a freshwater crocodile.
  • They can leap high out of the water to reach their prey if necessary.
  • They do not chew their food.
  • They eat meat.
  • They feed on a large variety of prey such as small mammals, birds and even domestic livestock.
  • They swallow it in large chunks.

Basketball

  • A player cannot run with the ball.
  • Basketball was invented in December 1891 by the Canadian clergyman, educator and Doctor James Naismith.
  • Basketball is usually played on an indoor court with two competing teams.
  • Basketball is one of the most popular spectator as well as participant sports in the world.
  • By 1897-1898, teams of five became standard.
  • Five players each attempt to score points or “baskets” by throwing an inflated ball so that it descends through one of two baskets suspended at each end of the court above their heads.
  • In 1891 teams had nine players, and the goals were wooden peach baskets affixed to the walls.
  • In the early days, basketball involved elements of American football, Association football and hockey and the first ball used was a football.
  • Naismith introduced the game when he was an instructor at the Young Men’s Christian Association (YMCA) Training School in Springfield, Massachusetts, where he organised a vigorous recreation suitable for indoor winter play.
  • No shouldering, holding, pushing, tripping or striking in any way the person of an opponent shall be allowed.
  • The ball may be thrown in any direction with one or both hands.
  • The team scoring the most “baskets”, through field goals or foul shots, wins the game.
  • US servicemen in World War II popularised the sport in many other countries
