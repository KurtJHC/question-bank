{^ A ^}
It was beautiful spring weather, but neither dogs nor humans were aware of it. Each day the sun rose earlier and set later. It was dawn by three in the morning, and twilight lingered till nine at night. The whole long day was a blaze of sunshine. The ghostly winter silence had given way to the great spring murmur of awakening life. This murmur arose from all the land, fraught with the joy of living. It came from the things that lived and moved again, things which had been as dead and which had not moved during the long months of frost. The sap was rising in the pines. The willows and aspens were bursting out in young buds. Shrubs and vines were putting on fresh garbs of green. Crickets sang in the nights, and in the days all manner of creeping, crawling things rustled forth into the sun. Partridges and woodpeckers were booming and knocking in the forest. Squirrels were chattering, birds singing, and overhead honked the wild-fowl driving up from the south in cunning wedges that split the air.

From every hill slope came the trickle of running water, the music of unseen fountains. All things were thawing, bending, snapping. The Yukon was straining to break loose the ice that bound it down. It ate away from beneath; the sun ate from above. Air-holes formed, fissures sprang and spread apart, while thin sections of ice fell through bodily into the water. And amid all this bursting, rending, throbbing of awakening life, under the blazing sun and through the soft-sighing breezes, like wayfarers to death, staggered the two men, the woman, and the huskies.

With the dogs falling, Mercedes weeping and riding the sled, Hal complaining bitterly, and Charles’s eyes wistfully watering, they staggered into John Thornton’s camp at the mouth of White River. When they halted, the dogs dropped down as though they had all been struck dead. Mercedes dried her eyes and looked at John Thornton. Charles sat down on a log to rest. He sat down very slowly and painstakingly what of his great stiffness. Hal did the talking. John Thornton was whittling the last touches on an axe-handle he had made from a stick of birch. He whittled and listened, gave monosyllabic replies, and, when it was asked, terse advice. He knew the breed, and he gave his advice in the certainty that it would not be followed.

“They told us up above that the bottom was dropping out of the trail and that the best thing for us to do was to lay over,” Hal said in response to Thornton’s warning to take no more chances on the rotten ice. “They told us we couldn’t make White River, and here we are.” This last with a sneering ring of triumph in it.

“And they told you true,” John Thornton answered. “The bottom’s likely to drop out at any moment. Only fools, with the blind luck of fools, could have made it. I tell you straight, I wouldn’t risk my carcass on that ice for all the gold in Alaska.”

Health Benefits of owning a Dog

With concern growing about the level of obesity in children and adults, the role of dogs in increasing exercise levels has become an important area of research. There is a growing body of evidence demonstrating that the improved health status of dog owners may, in part, be explained by the increased walking facilitated by dog ownership.

A study of over 14,000 pregnant women in England found a positive association between participation in activity at least once a week and dog ownership. In fact, dog owners were 50% more likely to achieve the recommended three hours of activity most days of the week. Dog owners were also more likely to participate in brisk walking.

Australian researcher, Dr Hayley Christian identified that people who acquired a dog increased their recreational walking by 48 minutes per week compared with an increase of only 12 minutes per week for people who did not acquire a dog during the period of the study. She also found that dog owners are more likely to meet the recommended levels of exercise than non-dog owners.

Western cultures report increasing rates of asthma and allergic disease, with pets sometimes implicated as a causal factor. An interesting research development in recent years however, has demonstrated the opposite may in fact be the case; the presence of cats and dogs in the home from an early age may actually ‘acclimatise’ the developing immune system so that it is less sensitive to allergens in later life. The presence of pets in the home clearly has some stabilising influence on the immune functioning of children.

New studies found that exposure to pets, especially dogs, soon after birth may reduce the development of allergic disease in children without a family history of allergy. One study states that having a dog in infancy is associated with reduced allergic sensitivity. Dr June McNicholas from the UK found that children from pet owning families have less school absenteeism through illness, and are more likely to have normal levels of immune function than children from non-owning families. Dr McNicholas measured levels of salivary immunoglobulin A in children of pet owning and non-pet owning households. It was found that pet ownership was significantly associated with better attendance rates across all primary / junior classes at school, but was especially evident in lower school classes (ages 5-8 years).

     Loses itself.

Clear, the sheathed bodies slick as seals
     Loosen and tingle;
And by the board the bare foot feels
     The suck of shingle.

 

They paddle in the shallows still;
     Two splash each other; 
Then all swim out to wait until
     The right waves gather.

Some of these trails still exist, giving hikers the opportunity to explore the landscape of the Yukon.

16  ………..  Strictly speaking, there are multiple "Yukon Trails," or routes used by prospectors and settlers seeking access to the depths of the Yukon during the Klondike Gold Rush. These include the Overland Trail in Canada and the international Chilkoot Trail. No modern trail in the Yukon actually bears the name "The Yukon Trail."

17  ………..  Chilkoot Trail is possibly the most accessible because it connects the US Klondike Gold Rush National Historic Park with Canada's Chilkoot Trail National Historic Site – both ends of the trail are in a national park facility. Although the trail is only 33 miles long, its difficult terrain and strenuous nature make hiking it a backpacking and camping expedition. According to the Parks Canada, just hiking the almost 8 miles from Sheep Camp to Happy Camp takes 12 hours.

18  ………..  Another Yukon trail is the Dawson Overland Trail, perhaps best known for its part of the famed Yukon Quest dog-sledding race. The modern trail runs 70 miles from Whitehorse and Braeburn Lodge, entirely within the Canadian Yukon. However, the classic Overland Trail ran all the way to Dawson City, near the US-Canadian border, and from there into Alaska. This route started as a dog-sledding freight path and is now used by hikers, horseback riders, dog-sledders and snowmobilers.

19  ………..  The Yukon is a habitat for both grizzly and black bears, so safety precautions must be followed. The National Park Service requests that all hikers on Chilkoot Trail use their bear-safe food containers and camp only in designated areas. If this is not possible, hang your food in its container some distance from the camp using a rope and a bear pole, so it is both out of reach and a safe distance away. Make plenty of noise while hiking to give bears warning of your approach.

Some of the Yukon Trails are slowly being visited more often.
Accommodating natural creatures and humans in the same environment is not always easy.                   
Isolated and relatively untouched is the best way to describe the Yukon Trails.
The Yukon is often considered to be part of the Rocky Mountains but it is not. 
Sub-zero hiking in the Yukon is not for the faint-hearted or inexperienced.
The Yukon Trail should be spoken of in the plural form.    
Largely through Jack London’s stories, the Yukon has been associated with dogs and sleds.

20  ………..  Due to frigid temperatures, heavy snowfall and the risk of avalanche, hiking on the Chilkoot Trail in winter is not recommended by either US or Canadian park authorities. Snows persist in the upper reaches of the trail well into June, so the Chilkoot hiking season is between mid-June and late September. The Dawson route, on the other hand, is open all year to hikers using snowshoes.
