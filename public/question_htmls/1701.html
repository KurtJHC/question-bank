{^ Extract C ^}
We've all heard of holidays from hell. But what about going on a holiday to The Hell, a town tucked away in the Swartberg mountains in South Africa? Its other name is Gamkaskloof. This secret and secluded place is a hidden gem often by-passed by travellers who are put off either by its name, or by the rather treacherous but beautiful, one-and-only road that would take them there. This road was built almost single handedly by Koos Van Zyl and his bulldozer in 1963. Prior to this, the small community of settlers who lived there were isolated from the outside world. Legend has it that, only two years after World War One had finished, did the people in The Hell find out there had been a world war. But once the road opened many left for the comparable bright lights of neighbouring towns. The natural beauty of The Hell, located in a magnificent kloof, walled in by majestic mountains (combined with fascinating history as recounted by the locals, should you stop for a chat) makes The Hell a special place worth visiting for those who enjoy leaving the beaten track. So, how did The Hell get its nickname? Certainly not from its own inhabitants! The general explanation is from British tax collectors and/or stock inspectors who had to endure the arduous voyage there and back in the line of duty. And I am sure, back then with no road it really did feel like a hellish trip! Or maybe it was from the soldiers who used to suffer an across-the-mountains endurance trip to The Hell and back.

The Australian town of Coober Pedy looks like something straight out of a movie—probably because it is. It has also forced the town’s residents underground—literally. The main attractions of the city include the dugouts, houses, churches, and 4-star hotels built underground. Nothing about Coober Pedy is for the faint of heart. For starters, it’s hot—really hot. In the summer, temperatures creep up to 45°C. Before the city passed a tree-planting initiative encouraging residents to plant seeds around town, its tallest tree was a sculpture built from scraps of metal. Even grass is considered a commodity in Coober Pedy. The thin veil of red dust that settles on roadways, cars and buildings is a reminder of Coober Pedy’s strange charm. There really is no other place like it on—or below—Earth.

We've all heard of holidays from hell. But what about going on a holiday to The Hell, a town tucked away in the Swartberg mountains in South Africa? Its other name is Gamkaskloof. This secret and secluded place is a hidden gem often by-passed by travellers who are put off either by its name, or by the rather treacherous but beautiful, one-and-only road that would take them there. This road was built almost single handedly by Koos Van Zyl and his bulldozer in 1963. Prior to this, the small community of settlers who lived there were isolated from the outside world. Legend has it that, only two years after World War One had finished, did the people in The Hell find out there had been a world war. But once the road opened many left for the comparable bright lights of neighbouring towns. The natural beauty of The Hell, located in a magnificent kloof, walled in by majestic mountains (combined with fascinating history as recounted by the locals, should you stop for a chat) makes The Hell a special place worth visiting for those who enjoy leaving the beaten track. So, how did The Hell get its nickname? Certainly not from its own inhabitants! The general explanation is from British tax collectors and/or stock inspectors who had to endure the arduous voyage there and back in the line of duty. And I am sure, back then with no road it really did feel like a hellish trip! Or maybe it was from the soldiers who used to suffer an across-the-mountains endurance trip to The Hell and back.

The Australian town of Coober Pedy looks like something straight out of a movie—probably because it is. In 1985, Mel Gibson, Tina Turner and a team of filmmakers descended onto this barren mining town in the South Australian Outback to shoot ‘Mad Max Beyond Thunderdome’. The otherworldly landscape, which is checkered with ruddy-coloured mounds of sandstone was the perfect backdrop for the post-apocalyptic movie. It has also forced the town’s residents underground—literally. The main attractions of the city include the dugouts, houses, churches, and 4-star hotels built underground. Nothing about Coober Pedy is for the faint of heart. For starters, it’s hot—really hot. In the summer, temperatures creep up to 45°C. Before the city passed a tree-planting initiative encouraging residents to plant seeds around town, its tallest tree was a sculpture built from scraps of metal. Even grass is considered a commodity in Coober Pedy. The thin veil of red dust that settles on roadways, cars and buildings is a reminder of Coober Pedy’s strange charm. There really is no other place like it on—or below—Earth.
